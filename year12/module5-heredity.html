Module 5: Heredity

How reproduction, cell division and DNA replication combine to pass genetic information from one generation to the next, and how patterns of inheritance can be predicted and explained.

Syllabus dot points

Reproduction

Inquiry question: What is the relationship between the structure and function of DNA?

  • Model the processes involved in cell replication, including mitosis and meiosis, and DNA replication, and their significance.
  • Analyse the mechanism of cell replication that ensures the continuity of species from one generation to the next.
  • Investigate the role of the enzymes helicase and polymerase, and other factors, in the process of DNA replication.

Cell division

Inquiry question: How does reproduction assure the continuity of a species?

  • Model how the sorting and combination of alleles at fertilisation can result in genetic variation.
  • Assess the importance of meiosis in producing genetic variation, including consideration of crossing over and independent assortment.

Patterns of inheritance

Inquiry question: How is the information for regulating cellular activities passed on to the next generation?

  • Predict the outcomes of mono-hybrid and dihybrid crosses, including test crosses, using Punnett squares and/or pedigree analysis.
  • Investigate patterns of inheritance, including autosomal and sex-linked inheritance, incomplete dominance, codominance and multiple alleles.

Genetic variation

Inquiry question: Why is the study of the genetic material passed on to offspring important?

  • Assess the effect of the environment on the phenotypic expression of a genotype.
  • Evaluate, using examples, the importance of the interaction between genotype and environment in the determination of phenotype.

Notes

Mitosis vs meiosis

Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells and is used for growth and repair. Meiosis produces four genetically different haploid gametes and is used for sexual reproduction. The distinction students most often lose marks on: meiosis involves two divisions (meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes, meiosis II separates sister chromatids), and it is meiosis I — specifically crossing over during prophase I, and independent assortment during metaphase I — that generates genetic variation.

DNA replication

DNA replication is semi-conservative: each new double helix contains one original (template) strand and one newly synthesised strand. Helicase unwinds and separates the double helix by breaking hydrogen bonds between complementary bases; DNA polymerase then synthesises the new strand by adding complementary nucleotides, working in the 5' to 3' direction only — this is why replication is continuous on the leading strand and discontinuous (Okazaki fragments) on the lagging strand.

Patterns of inheritance

Most exam marks in this section come from correctly setting up and interpreting a Punnett square or pedigree, not from memorising vocabulary. Be precise with terminology: genotype (the allele combination) vs phenotype (the observable trait); homozygous vs heterozygous; dominant vs recessive. For sex-linked traits, remember the gene is carried on the X chromosome, so males (XY) only need one recessive allele to express the trait — this is why conditions like red-green colour blindness are more common in males.

Genotype–environment interaction

Phenotype is not determined by genotype alone. Classic HSC examples: Himalayan rabbits express dark fur only in cooler extremities because the enzyme responsible for pigment production is temperature-sensitive; and human height reflects both genetic potential and environmental factors like nutrition. Students should be able to argue this both ways — that genotype sets a range of possible phenotypes, and environment determines where within that range the actual phenotype falls.

Practice questions

  • 2 marks

    Q1 (Recall). Distinguish between the products of mitosis and the products of meiosis.

    Show suggested answer

    Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells, used for growth and repair. Meiosis produces four genetically different haploid daughter cells (gametes), used for sexual reproduction.

  • 4 marks

    Q2 (Application). In pea plants, tall (T) is dominant to short (t). A heterozygous tall plant is crossed with a short plant. Using a Punnett square, predict the genotype and phenotype ratios of the offspring.

    Show suggested answer

    Cross: Tt × tt. Gametes from the heterozygous parent: T and t. Gametes from the short parent: t and t. Offspring genotypes: Tt, Tt, tt, tt — a 1:1 ratio of Tt to tt. Phenotype ratio: 1 tall : 1 short (50% tall, 50% short).

  • 6 marks

    Q3 (Extended response). Explain how meiosis contributes to genetic variation in a population, and evaluate why this variation is significant for a species.

    Show suggested answer

    Meiosis generates variation through two main mechanisms during meiosis I: crossing over, where homologous chromosomes exchange segments of genetic material during prophase I, creating new allele combinations on each chromosome; and independent assortment, where homologous chromosome pairs align randomly at the metaphase plate, meaning each gamete receives a random mix of maternal and paternal chromosomes. Combined with the random fertilisation of gametes at conception, these mechanisms mean that offspring are genetically distinct from their parents and siblings. This variation is significant because it provides the raw material on which natural selection can act — a genetically diverse population is more likely to contain individuals with traits suited to changing environmental conditions, improving the species' chances of long-term survival.
