Module 5: Heredity

How reproduction, cell division and DNA replication combine to pass genetic information from one generation to the next, and how patterns of inheritance can be predicted and explained.

Syllabus checklist

Reproduction

Inquiry question: What is the relationship between the structure and function of DNA?

  • Model the processes involved in cell replication, including mitosis and meiosis, and DNA replication, and their significance.
  • Analyse the mechanism of cell replication that ensures the continuity of species from one generation to the next.
  • Investigate the role of the enzymes helicase and polymerase, and other factors, in the process of DNA replication.

Cell division

Inquiry question: How does reproduction assure the continuity of a species?

  • Model how the sorting and combination of alleles at fertilisation can result in genetic variation.
  • Assess the importance of meiosis in producing genetic variation, including consideration of crossing over and independent assortment.

Patterns of inheritance

Inquiry question: How is the information for regulating cellular activities passed on to the next generation?

  • Predict the outcomes of mono-hybrid and dihybrid crosses, including test crosses, using Punnett squares and/or pedigree analysis.
  • Investigate patterns of inheritance, including autosomal and sex-linked inheritance, incomplete dominance, codominance and multiple alleles.

Genetic variation

Inquiry question: Why is the study of the genetic material passed on to offspring important?

  • Assess the effect of the environment on the phenotypic expression of a genotype.
  • Evaluate, using examples, the importance of the interaction between genotype and environment in the determination of phenotype.

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. Mitosis, meiosis and DNA replication — modelling the processes

Mitosis is a single cell division producing two genetically identical diploid daughter cells (containing the same chromosome number as the parent cell), used for growth and repair of body tissue. Its stages are prophase (chromosomes condense and become visible), metaphase (chromosomes align at the cell's equator), anaphase (sister chromatids are pulled to opposite poles) and telophase (two new nuclei form, followed by cytokinesis dividing the cytoplasm). Meiosis involves two successive divisions and produces four genetically different haploid daughter cells (gametes, with half the chromosome number), used for sexual reproduction. Both processes must be preceded by DNA replication in interphase, ensuring each daughter cell receives a complete copy of the genome.

2. Cell replication and continuity of species

Mitosis maintains genetic continuity within an organism across its lifetime — every somatic (body) cell produced retains the same genome as the original zygote, allowing coordinated growth, tissue repair, and asexual reproduction in some organisms. Meiosis, paired with fertilisation, maintains the chromosome number across generations in sexually reproducing species: without meiosis halving the chromosome number in gametes, the chromosome number would double with every generation. This combination — meiosis halving the chromosome number, fertilisation restoring it — is the mechanism that ensures a species' characteristic chromosome number remains stable indefinitely while still allowing genetic variation between individuals.

3. Enzymes in DNA replication

DNA replication is semi-conservative: each new double helix contains one original (template) strand and one newly synthesised strand. Helicase unwinds and separates the two strands of the double helix by breaking the hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs, creating a replication fork. DNA polymerase then synthesises the new complementary strand by adding free nucleotides according to base-pairing rules (A–T, C–G), working only in the 5' to 3' direction. Because the two template strands run in opposite directions (antiparallel), one new strand (the leading strand) is synthesised continuously in the same direction as the replication fork opens, while the other (the lagging strand) is synthesised discontinuously in short segments called Okazaki fragments, which are later joined by the enzyme DNA ligase. Other relevant factors include primase (lays down a short RNA primer to allow polymerase to begin) and the overall high fidelity of the process, which minimises replication errors.

4. Sorting and combination of alleles at fertilisation

At fertilisation, one haploid gamete from each parent combines to form a diploid zygote, meaning the resulting offspring inherits one set of alleles from the mother and one from the father for every gene. Because each parent's gametes are already genetically varied (due to crossing over and independent assortment during meiosis — see below), and because fertilisation itself is essentially random (which particular sperm fertilises which particular egg), the combination of alleles in any given offspring is highly unlikely to be repeated exactly, even among siblings. This randomness at fertilisation is an additional source of genetic variation layered on top of the variation already generated during meiosis.

5. Meiosis, crossing over and independent assortment

Meiosis generates genetic variation primarily through two mechanisms occurring during meiosis I. Crossing over occurs during prophase I, when homologous chromosomes (one maternal, one paternal copy of each chromosome) pair up and exchange corresponding segments of genetic material at points called chiasmata — this creates new combinations of alleles on a single chromosome that did not exist in either parent. Independent assortment occurs during metaphase I, when homologous chromosome pairs align randomly at the metaphase plate — because the orientation of each pair is independent of the others, the specific combination of maternal and paternal chromosomes that ends up in each gamete varies enormously (for humans, with 23 chromosome pairs, independent assortment alone can produce over 8 million possible chromosome combinations per gamete). Together with random fertilisation, these mechanisms explain why sexually reproducing offspring are genetically distinct from both parents and from each other.

6. Predicting monohybrid and dihybrid crosses

A Punnett square predicts the possible genotype (and resulting phenotype) combinations of offspring from a cross between two known parental genotypes, by systematically combining each possible parental gamete. A monohybrid cross tracks a single gene/trait (e.g. Tt × Tt); a dihybrid cross tracks two genes/traits simultaneously, typically assuming independent assortment (e.g. RrYy × RrYy, producing a 9:3:3:1 phenotype ratio when both genes show simple dominance). A test cross is used to determine an unknown genotype: an individual showing the dominant phenotype (who could be homozygous or heterozygous) is crossed with a known homozygous recessive individual — if any offspring show the recessive phenotype, the unknown parent must have been heterozygous. Pedigree analysis uses a family tree diagram (with standard symbols for affected/unaffected males and females) to trace the inheritance pattern of a trait across generations, allowing inference of dominant/recessive, autosomal/sex-linked inheritance patterns from the pattern of affected individuals.

7. Patterns of inheritance: autosomal, sex-linked, incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles

Autosomal inheritance involves genes located on the autosomes (non-sex chromosomes), following standard dominant/recessive patterns regardless of the offspring's sex. Sex-linked inheritance involves genes carried on the X chromosome (the Y chromosome carries very few genes) — because males (XY) have only one X chromosome, a single recessive allele is enough for them to express a sex-linked recessive trait, which is why conditions such as red-green colour blindness and haemophilia are far more common in males than females. Incomplete dominance occurs when neither allele is fully dominant, producing a heterozygous phenotype that is an intermediate blend of both homozygous phenotypes (e.g. red × white snapdragon flowers producing pink offspring). Codominance occurs when both alleles are fully and simultaneously expressed in the heterozygote, rather than blending (e.g. a roan coat colour in cattle showing distinct patches of both red and white hair, or human ABO blood type where the A and B alleles are both codominant to each other). Multiple alleles describes a gene with more than two possible allele variants in the population, even though any individual only carries two — the human ABO blood group system (alleles Iᴸ, IᲉ, i) is the standard example.

8. Environmental effects on phenotypic expression

Phenotype is not determined by genotype alone — environmental conditions can influence how a genotype is expressed. A classic example is the Himalayan rabbit (and Siamese cat), which carries a temperature-sensitive allele for coat colour: the enzyme responsible for producing dark pigment only functions properly at cooler temperatures, so fur is dark on the cooler extremities (ears, nose, paws, tail) and pale on the warmer body core, even though the genotype is identical across the whole animal. Human examples include height and body weight, which reflect both genetic potential and environmental factors such as nutrition, and skin tone, which can vary with UV exposure (tanning) around a genetically determined baseline.

9. Evaluating genotype–environment interaction

A genotype typically sets a range of possible phenotypes (a "reaction norm") rather than a single fixed outcome, and the environment determines where within that range an individual's actual phenotype falls. This has significant practical implications: in agriculture, the same crop genotype can produce different yields depending on soil quality, water and fertiliser availability; in medicine, some genetic predispositions (e.g. to type 2 diabetes) are only expressed as disease given particular lifestyle/environmental triggers. A strong "evaluate" response for this dot point argues both directions — genotype constrains what's biologically possible, but environment substantially determines what's actually observed — rather than treating the two as separate, competing explanations.

Glossary

Allele
A version of a gene; different alleles of the same gene can produce different phenotypes.
Codominance
A pattern of inheritance where both alleles in a heterozygote are fully and separately expressed.
Crossing over
The exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis, increasing genetic variation.
Diploid
Having two complete sets of chromosomes (one from each parent), written 2n.
Genotype
The specific allele combination an organism carries for a gene or set of genes.
Haploid
Having one complete set of chromosomes, written n; characteristic of gametes.
Incomplete dominance
A pattern of inheritance where the heterozygous phenotype is an intermediate blend of both homozygous phenotypes.
Independent assortment
The random alignment of homologous chromosome pairs during metaphase I of meiosis, increasing genetic variation.
Phenotype
The observable physical or biochemical characteristics of an organism, resulting from genotype and environment.
Semi-conservative replication
The process by which each new DNA molecule contains one original strand and one newly synthesised strand.
Sex-linked inheritance
Inheritance of a gene located on a sex chromosome (typically the X chromosome).

Practice questions

  • 2 marks

    Q1 Distinguish Distinguish between the products of mitosis and the products of meiosis.

    Show suggested answer

    Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells, used for growth and repair. Meiosis produces four genetically different haploid daughter cells (gametes), used for sexual reproduction.

  • 4 marks

    Q2 Predict In pea plants, tall (T) is dominant to short (t). A heterozygous tall plant is crossed with a short plant. Using a Punnett square, predict the genotype and phenotype ratios of the offspring.

    Show suggested answer

    Cross: Tt × tt. Gametes from the heterozygous parent: T and t. Gametes from the short parent: t and t. Offspring genotypes: Tt, Tt, tt, tt — a 1:1 ratio of Tt to tt. Phenotype ratio: 1 tall : 1 short (50% tall, 50% short).

  • 6 marks

    Q3 Explain / Evaluate Explain how meiosis contributes to genetic variation in a population, and evaluate why this variation is significant for a species.

    Show suggested answer

    Meiosis generates variation through two main mechanisms during meiosis I: crossing over, where homologous chromosomes exchange segments of genetic material during prophase I, creating new allele combinations on each chromosome; and independent assortment, where homologous chromosome pairs align randomly at the metaphase plate, meaning each gamete receives a random mix of maternal and paternal chromosomes. Combined with the random fertilisation of gametes at conception, these mechanisms mean that offspring are genetically distinct from their parents and siblings. This variation is significant because it provides the raw material on which natural selection can act — a genetically diverse population is more likely to contain individuals with traits suited to changing environmental conditions, improving the species' chances of long-term survival.
