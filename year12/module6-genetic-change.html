Module 6: Genetic Change

How mutation introduces new genetic variation, and how biotechnology and genetic technologies are used to deliberately induce genetic change — and the implications of doing so.

Syllabus checklist

Mutation

Inquiry question: How does genetic change occur?

  • Explain how a range of mutagens operate, including their effects on the structure and function of organisms.
  • Assess the causes and effects of genetic variation, including gene mutation, chromosomal rearrangement, and biotechnological applications.

Biotechnology

Inquiry question: Does artificial manipulation of DNA have the potential to change populations forever?

  • Investigate the uses and advantages of current genetic technologies that induce genetic change.
  • Compare the processes and outcomes of reproductive technologies, including but not limited to selective breeding, gene cloning, and genetic engineering.
  • Evaluate the benefits of using genetic technologies in agricultural, medical and industrial applications.

Genetic technologies

Inquiry question: What impact has an understanding of genetics had on society?

  • Interpret a range of secondary sources to assess the influence of social, economic and cultural contexts on a range of genetic technologies.
  • Evaluate the social implications and ethical uses of genetic knowledge, including gene therapy, cloning, genetic screening, and genetically modified organisms.

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. How mutagens operate

A mutagen is a physical, chemical or biological agent that increases the rate of mutation above the natural background rate. Physical mutagens include ionising radiation (X-rays, gamma rays) and ultraviolet light, which directly damage DNA structure — UV light, for example, causes adjacent thymine bases to bond incorrectly (thymine dimers), distorting the DNA helix and causing replication errors if not repaired. Chemical mutagens include substances that alter base structure or pairing (e.g. certain compounds found in tobacco smoke, which can cause base substitutions), and intercalating agents that insert themselves between DNA bases, causing insertion or deletion errors during replication. Biological mutagens include some viruses, which insert their own genetic material into a host's DNA, potentially disrupting genes at the insertion site. Cells have DNA repair mechanisms that fix most mutagen-induced damage, but repair is not always successful, and errors that survive replication become permanent mutations.

2. Causes and effects of genetic variation

A gene mutation is a change in the nucleotide sequence within a single gene. Substitutions replace one base with another, which may be silent (no change to the resulting amino acid, due to the redundancy of the genetic code), missense (produces a different amino acid, potentially altering protein function), or nonsense (creates a premature stop codon, usually severely disrupting the protein). Insertions and deletions can cause a frameshift mutation, shifting the reading frame for every codon downstream of the mutation and usually producing a completely non-functional protein. A chromosomal mutation affects the structure or number of whole chromosomes, including deletions (loss of a chromosome segment), duplications (a segment repeated), inversions (a segment reversed in orientation), translocations (a segment moved to a different chromosome), and non-disjunction — the failure of chromosomes to separate correctly during meiosis, which can result in a gamete with an abnormal chromosome number (e.g. trisomy 21, causing Down syndrome). Not all mutations are harmful: many are neutral (no effect on fitness), and a small proportion are beneficial, providing the raw genetic variation on which natural selection acts.

3. Current genetic technologies that induce genetic change

Modern genetic technologies deliberately introduce genetic change for a defined purpose. Key examples include recombinant DNA technology (inserting a gene from one organism into another, e.g. inserting the human insulin gene into bacteria so they produce human insulin), gene editing tools such as CRISPR-Cas9 (which allow precise, targeted cutting and editing of DNA sequences at a specific location in the genome), and gene therapy (introducing a functional copy of a gene into a patient's cells to correct or compensate for a faulty gene). Advantages of current technologies over older methods include speed (results in one generation rather than many), precision (targeting a specific gene rather than relying on whole-organism breeding outcomes), and the ability to move genetic material across species boundaries that would never interbreed naturally.

4. Selective breeding, gene cloning and genetic engineering compared

Selective breeding works only with genetic variation already present within a species — breeders choose individuals with desirable traits to reproduce, gradually shifting the population's traits over many generations; it is slow, limited to existing variation, and can reduce genetic diversity (inbreeding depression) if practised too intensively. Gene cloning (in the biotechnology sense) involves producing multiple identical copies of a specific gene, often by inserting it into a bacterial plasmid that replicates the gene as the bacteria divide — this is a key step in producing genetically engineered products at scale. Genetic engineering directly manipulates an organism's DNA: restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific recognition sequences (producing "sticky ends"), DNA ligase joins a gene of interest into a vector (commonly a bacterial plasmid), and the resulting recombinant DNA is introduced into a host cell, which then expresses the new gene. Genetic engineering is faster than selective breeding and can introduce genes from entirely unrelated species, but raises additional ethical, biosafety and regulatory questions that selective breeding does not.

5. Evaluating benefits in agriculture, medicine and industry

Agricultural applications include pest-resistant crops (e.g. Bt crops engineered to produce a natural insecticidal protein), herbicide-tolerant crops, and drought- or salinity-tolerant varieties, which can increase yield stability and food security. Medical applications include genetically engineered production of human insulin and other therapeutic proteins (replacing older, less reliable animal-derived sources), gene therapy for inherited disorders, and genetic screening for disease risk. Industrial applications include engineered microorganisms that produce enzymes for detergents, biofuels, or food processing more efficiently than natural strains. A genuine evaluation weighs these benefits against risks and limitations — e.g. the possibility of unintended ecological effects from gene flow into wild populations, cost and accessibility barriers to genetic medicine, and the need for ongoing regulatory oversight — rather than presenting only the advantages.

6. Social, economic and cultural influences on genetic technologies

The development and adoption of genetic technologies is shaped by more than just scientific capability. Social factors include public trust and acceptance (e.g. resistance to genetically modified food in some markets despite scientific consensus on safety), and access to genetic testing or therapy varying by socioeconomic status. Economic factors include the cost of developing and regulating new technologies, and the commercial interests of biotechnology companies (e.g. patenting of GM seed varieties, which can affect farmers' seed-saving practices). Cultural and religious factors include differing views across communities about the acceptability of manipulating genetic material, particularly in relation to human genetic technologies. Interpreting secondary sources for this dot point means being able to identify bias, evaluate the reliability of the source, and distinguish factual claims from opinion when researching how a genetic technology has been received by society.

7. Ethical and social implications of genetic knowledge

Gene therapy raises questions about long-term safety, equitable access, and the distinction between treating disease (broadly accepted) versus genetic enhancement for non-medical traits (more contested). Cloning (producing a genetically identical copy of an organism) raises concerns about genetic diversity, welfare of cloned animals (historically associated with higher rates of health problems), and — for the hypothetical case of human cloning — significant ethical objections regarding identity and consent. Genetic screening (testing for disease-associated genes, prenatally or otherwise) raises questions about privacy, potential discrimination (e.g. by employers or insurers), and the psychological impact of knowing one's genetic risk for an untreatable condition. Genetically modified organisms (GMOs) raise ecological questions (gene flow to wild relatives, impact on non-target species) alongside the economic and cultural questions discussed above. A well-structured "evaluate" response addresses a specific, named technology rather than genetic technology in general, and reaches a clear, reasoned conclusion rather than simply listing considerations.

Glossary

Frameshift mutation
An insertion or deletion mutation that shifts the reading frame of all codons downstream of the mutation site.
Gene therapy
The introduction of a functional gene into a patient's cells to treat or compensate for a genetic disorder.
Genetic engineering
The direct, deliberate alteration of an organism's DNA, often by inserting a gene from another organism.
Missense mutation
A substitution mutation that changes the resulting amino acid in a protein.
Mutagen
A physical, chemical or biological agent that increases the rate of mutation.
Non-disjunction
The failure of chromosomes to separate correctly during meiosis, resulting in gametes with an abnormal chromosome number.
Recombinant DNA
DNA formed by combining genetic material from two different sources, e.g. via genetic engineering.
Selective breeding
Choosing organisms with desirable traits to reproduce, gradually shifting a population's traits over generations.
Vector (genetics)
A vehicle (e.g. a bacterial plasmid) used to carry a gene of interest into a host cell.

Practice questions

  • 3 marks

    Q1 Distinguish Distinguish between a gene mutation and a chromosomal mutation, providing an example of each.

    Show suggested answer

    A gene mutation is a change in the nucleotide sequence within a single gene — e.g. a point mutation causing sickle cell anaemia, where a single base substitution alters one amino acid in the haemoglobin protein. A chromosomal mutation involves a change in the structure or number of whole chromosomes — e.g. non-disjunction during meiosis resulting in trisomy 21 (Down syndrome), where an individual has an extra copy of chromosome 21.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 Outline Outline the difference between selective breeding and genetic engineering as methods of inducing genetic change.

    Show suggested answer

    Selective breeding involves choosing individuals with desirable traits to reproduce over many generations, working only with genetic variation already present within the species. Genetic engineering directly alters an organism's DNA, often by inserting a gene from a different organism using tools such as restriction enzymes and vectors, allowing much faster and more targeted genetic change than selective breeding can achieve.

  • 7 marks

    Q3 Evaluate Evaluate the use of genetically modified crops in agriculture.

    Show suggested answer

    Genetically modified (GM) crops can be engineered for traits such as pest resistance, herbicide tolerance, or drought tolerance, which can increase crop yields and reduce reliance on chemical pesticides — for example, Bt cotton, engineered to produce a bacterial toxin, has reduced insecticide use in some growing regions. GM crops can also improve food security by increasing yield stability under adverse conditions, which is significant given a growing global population and changing climate. However, there are legitimate concerns: the long-term ecological effects of gene flow from GM crops to wild relatives are not fully understood; reliance on a narrow range of GM varieties may reduce agricultural biodiversity; and there are economic and ethical concerns, such as smaller farmers becoming dependent on seed suppliers who hold patents on GM seed lines. Overall, GM crops offer clear agricultural and food security benefits, but responsible use requires ongoing ecological monitoring and consideration of the socioeconomic impact on farming communities, meaning the technology's value depends heavily on how and where it is regulated and applied.
