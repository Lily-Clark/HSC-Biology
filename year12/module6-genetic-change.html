Module 6: Genetic Change

How mutation introduces new genetic variation, and how biotechnology and genetic technologies are used to deliberately induce genetic change — and the implications of doing so.

Syllabus dot points

Mutation

Inquiry question: How does genetic change occur?

  • Explain how a range of mutagens operate, including their effects on the structure and function of organisms.
  • Assess the causes and effects of genetic variation, including gene mutation, chromosomal rearrangement, and biotechnological applications.

Biotechnology

Inquiry question: Does artificial manipulation of DNA have the potential to change populations forever?

  • Investigate the uses and advantages of current genetic technologies that induce genetic change.
  • Compare the processes and outcomes of reproductive technologies, including but not limited to selective breeding, gene cloning, and genetic engineering.
  • Evaluate the benefits of using genetic technologies in agricultural, medical and industrial applications.

Genetic technologies

Inquiry question: What impact has an understanding of genetics had on society?

  • Interpret a range of secondary sources to assess the influence of social, economic and cultural contexts on a range of genetic technologies.
  • Evaluate the social implications and ethical uses of genetic knowledge, including gene therapy, cloning, genetic screening, and genetically modified organisms.

Notes

Types of mutation

A gene mutation is a change in the nucleotide sequence within a single gene: substitutions replace one base with another (which may be silent, missense, or nonsense depending on how the resulting codon changes); insertions and deletions can cause a frameshift, altering every codon that follows and usually producing a severely altered or non-functional protein. A chromosomal mutation affects the structure or number of whole chromosomes — including deletions, duplications, inversions, translocations, and non-disjunction (failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis, which can result in conditions such as trisomy 21).

Mutagens

Mutagens are physical, chemical or biological agents that increase the rate of mutation. Physical mutagens include ionising radiation (X-rays, UV light) which can directly damage DNA structure; chemical mutagens include substances that alter base pairing (e.g. certain compounds in tobacco smoke); biological mutagens include some viruses, which insert their genetic material into a host's DNA. Not all mutations are harmful — some are neutral, and a small proportion are beneficial, providing the raw genetic variation on which natural selection acts.

Selective breeding vs genetic engineering

Selective breeding works with existing genetic variation within a species — breeders choose individuals with desirable traits to reproduce, gradually shifting the population's traits over many generations, but it is slow and limited to variation already present in the gene pool. Genetic engineering directly manipulates an organism's DNA — for example, using restriction enzymes to cut DNA at specific sequences and DNA ligase to insert a gene of interest into a vector (such as a plasmid), which is then introduced into a host organism. This is faster and can introduce genes from entirely different species, but raises additional ethical and biosafety questions selective breeding does not.

Applications and implications

Genetic technologies have agricultural (e.g. pest- or drought-resistant crops), medical (e.g. genetically engineered insulin production, gene therapy for inherited disorders) and industrial applications (e.g. enzyme production). Evaluating these technologies means weighing genuine benefits (increased food security, treatment of disease) against risks and concerns (unintended ecological effects of GMOs, questions of consent and equity in genetic screening, and cultural or religious objections) — HSC-style "evaluate" questions expect both sides to be addressed, not just a list of advantages.

Practice questions

  • 3 marks

    Q1 (Application). Distinguish between a gene mutation and a chromosomal mutation, providing an example of each.

    Show suggested answer

    A gene mutation is a change in the nucleotide sequence within a single gene — e.g. a point mutation causing sickle cell anaemia, where a single base substitution alters one amino acid in the haemoglobin protein. A chromosomal mutation involves a change in the structure or number of whole chromosomes — e.g. non-disjunction during meiosis resulting in trisomy 21 (Down syndrome), where an individual has an extra copy of chromosome 21.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 (Recall). Outline the difference between selective breeding and genetic engineering as methods of inducing genetic change.

    Show suggested answer

    Selective breeding involves choosing individuals with desirable traits to reproduce over many generations, working only with genetic variation already present within the species. Genetic engineering directly alters an organism's DNA, often by inserting a gene from a different organism using tools such as restriction enzymes and vectors, allowing much faster and more targeted genetic change than selective breeding can achieve.

  • 7 marks

    Q3 (Extended response — Evaluate). Evaluate the use of genetically modified crops in agriculture.

    Show suggested answer

    Genetically modified (GM) crops can be engineered for traits such as pest resistance, herbicide tolerance, or drought tolerance, which can increase crop yields and reduce reliance on chemical pesticides — for example, Bt cotton, engineered to produce a bacterial toxin, has reduced insecticide use in some growing regions. GM crops can also improve food security by increasing yield stability under adverse conditions, which is significant given a growing global population and changing climate. However, there are legitimate concerns: the long-term ecological effects of gene flow from GM crops to wild relatives are not fully understood; reliance on a narrow range of GM varieties may reduce agricultural biodiversity; and there are economic and ethical concerns, such as smaller farmers becoming dependent on seed suppliers who hold patents on GM seed lines. Overall, GM crops offer clear agricultural and food security benefits, but responsible use requires ongoing ecological monitoring and consideration of the socioeconomic impact on farming communities, meaning the technology's value depends heavily on how and where it is regulated and applied.
