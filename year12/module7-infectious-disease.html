Module 7: Infectious Disease

The causes and transmission of infectious disease, how organisms defend against pathogens, and how science and public health strategies work to prevent and control disease spread.

Syllabus checklist

Causes of infectious disease

Inquiry question: What is a pathogen and how do the causes of infectious disease vary?

  • Describe the causes of infectious disease, including microorganisms, macroorganisms and non-cellular pathogens.
  • Investigate modes of transmission of infectious disease, including direct, indirect and vector transmission.

Responses to pathogens

Inquiry question: How does a species' response to a pathogen affect its ability to survive?

  • Analyse defence mechanisms of plants and animals against pathogens, including physical and chemical barriers, and the immune response (innate and adaptive immunity).
  • Model the innate and adaptive immune response and how immunity may be acquired, including naturally and artificially, actively and passively (e.g. vaccination).

Prevention, treatment and control

Inquiry question: How can the spread of infectious diseases be controlled?

  • Investigate procedures that can be employed to prevent the spread of disease, including quarantine, vector control, and hygiene practices.
  • Investigate and assess the effectiveness of pharmaceuticals, including antibiotics and antivirals, as treatment strategies for the control of infectious disease, and analyse the issue of antibiotic resistance.

Epidemiology

Inquiry question: How is the spread and control of a disease epidemic or pandemic managed?

  • Interpret data relating to the incidence and prevalence of infectious disease in populations.
  • Evaluate historical, culturally diverse and current strategies used to predict and control the spread of disease.

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. Causes of infectious disease

Pathogens fall into three broad categories. Microorganisms include bacteria (e.g. Salmonella, causing food poisoning, via toxin production or direct tissue damage), protozoa (e.g. Plasmodium, causing malaria) and pathogenic fungi (e.g. Tinea, causing ringworm). Macroorganisms are larger parasites, such as tapeworms and other helminths, which cause disease through nutrient competition or physical tissue damage. Non-cellular pathogens include viruses (e.g. influenza, HIV), which cannot reproduce independently and must hijack a host cell's machinery to replicate, and prions (misfolded proteins, e.g. causing Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease), which cause disease by triggering normal proteins to misfold in turn, without any genetic material involved at all — an unusual case among pathogens. Each pathogen type has a characteristic disease mechanism that should be linked to a named example in exam responses.

2. Modes of transmission

Direct transmission involves physical contact between an infected and a susceptible host, including skin-to-skin contact, droplet transmission over short range (e.g. coughing, sneezing), and sexual contact. Indirect transmission involves an intermediate medium, such as contaminated water or food (faecal-oral transmission), contaminated surfaces (fomites), or airborne particles that remain suspended and travel further than droplets. Vector transmission uses another living organism, most commonly an insect, to carry the pathogen from one host to another — e.g. Anopheles mosquitoes transmitting the Plasmodium parasite that causes malaria, or ticks transmitting Lyme disease bacteria. Correctly identifying the transmission route for a named disease is essential, because it directly determines which prevention and control strategy (e.g. vector control vs sanitation vs quarantine) will actually be effective.

3. Plant and animal defence mechanisms against pathogens

Plants rely mainly on physical barriers — a waxy cuticle on leaf surfaces, bark, and rigid cell walls — and chemical defences, including antimicrobial and antifungal compounds, and a localised hypersensitive response where cells around an infection site rapidly die to isolate and starve the pathogen of nutrients. Animals have layered defences. The first line comprises physical and chemical barriers: skin, mucous membranes, stomach acid, and lysozyme in tears and saliva. The second line is the innate immune response — a rapid, non-specific response including phagocytic white blood cells that engulf pathogens, the inflammatory response (increased blood flow and immune cell recruitment to an injury/infection site, causing redness, heat, swelling), and fever (raising body temperature to slow pathogen reproduction). The third line is the adaptive immune response — slower to activate but highly specific and capable of immunological memory, involving lymphocytes: B-cells that produce antibodies specific to a particular antigen, and T-cells that directly destroy infected cells or help coordinate the immune response.

4. Modelling immunity and how it is acquired

Immunity can be acquired in four distinct ways. Naturally active immunity develops when the body is exposed to a pathogen through actual infection, producing its own specific antibodies and memory cells. Naturally passive immunity occurs when antibodies are transferred from mother to infant, via the placenta before birth or through breast milk (colostrum) after birth, providing temporary protection while the infant's own immune system matures. Artificially active immunity is achieved through vaccination — exposure to a weakened, inactivated, or partial form of a pathogen (or, in newer mRNA vaccines, genetic instructions for the body to produce a pathogen protein) triggers the body's own immune response and generates memory cells, without causing the actual disease. Artificially passive immunity involves injecting pre-formed antibodies (e.g. antivenom, or antibody treatments for certain infections), providing immediate but only temporary protection, since the recipient's own immune system did not produce the antibodies and no memory cells are formed. High vaccination coverage across a population can also produce herd immunity, indirectly protecting unvaccinated individuals by reducing the pathogen's ability to spread through the population at all.

5. Preventing the spread of disease

Quarantine isolates infected or potentially infected individuals (or goods, e.g. at a national border) to prevent contact with susceptible populations while the disease is infectious. Vector control targets the intermediate organism carrying a disease — e.g. insecticide-treated bed nets and mosquito breeding site elimination to reduce malaria transmission. Hygiene practices — handwashing, safe food and water handling, sterilisation of medical equipment, and safe disposal of waste — reduce the likelihood of indirect and direct transmission. The most appropriate strategy depends on how a specific disease is transmitted, which is why correctly identifying the transmission mode (see above) is a prerequisite for proposing an effective prevention plan.

6. Pharmaceuticals and antibiotic resistance

Antibiotics target bacterial-specific structures or processes not present in human cells — for example, penicillin-type antibiotics interfere with bacterial cell wall synthesis, causing the bacterial cell to burst. Because bacteria and human cells differ fundamentally, antibiotics can kill bacteria with relatively little harm to the host; however, antibiotics are ineffective against viruses, which lack the cellular structures antibiotics target. Antivirals instead interfere with specific stages of the viral replication cycle (e.g. blocking viral entry into host cells, or blocking viral enzymes needed for replication), and tend to be more virus-specific than antibiotics are bacteria-specific. Antibiotic resistance arises through natural selection acting on bacterial populations: within a large bacterial population, random mutation may occasionally confer resistance to a given antibiotic; when the antibiotic is used, susceptible bacteria are killed while resistant individuals survive and reproduce without competition, so resistant strains become more common in the population over successive generations. Overuse and misuse of antibiotics (e.g. incomplete courses, use for viral infections, agricultural overuse) accelerates this process by increasing the frequency and intensity of the selection pressure applied.

7. Interpreting incidence and prevalence data

Incidence is the number (or rate) of new cases of a disease occurring in a population within a specified time period, and is used to track how quickly a disease is currently spreading. Prevalence is the total number (or proportion) of existing cases — new and pre-existing — within a population at a given point in time, and reflects the overall disease burden. A disease can have high incidence but low prevalence if cases resolve quickly (e.g. a fast-spreading but short-lived illness), or low incidence but high prevalence if cases persist for a long time (e.g. a chronic infection). When interpreting epidemiological graphs and tables, always specify the population and time period involved, and distinguish clearly between these two measures rather than using them interchangeably.

8. Historical and current strategies to predict and control disease spread

Historical strategies include early quarantine practices (used for centuries against diseases such as plague and smallpox), and the development of vaccination itself (from Edward Jenner's smallpox vaccine onward), which led to the eventual global eradication of smallpox through coordinated international vaccination campaigns. Current strategies include disease surveillance systems that track incidence data in real time to detect and respond to outbreaks early, contact tracing to identify and isolate exposed individuals before they can transmit further, and international coordination through bodies such as the World Health Organization for cross-border disease threats. Culturally diverse approaches include Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander protocols and traditional ecological knowledge that have historically informed disease management and continue to be recognised in the development of some contemporary Australian medicines and biological materials, alongside protections for Indigenous cultural and intellectual property. A strong "evaluate" response compares the effectiveness of a historical strategy with a current one for a named disease, rather than describing them in isolation.

Glossary

Adaptive immunity
A specific, memory-forming immune response involving lymphocytes, which develops over an organism's lifetime.
Antibiotic resistance
A bacterial population's reduced susceptibility to an antibiotic, arising through natural selection favouring resistant individuals.
Herd immunity
Indirect protection of unvaccinated individuals when enough of a population is immune to limit disease transmission.
Incidence
The number of new cases of a disease in a population over a specified time period.
Innate immunity
A rapid, non-specific immune defence present from birth.
Pathogen
A microorganism, macroorganism or non-cellular agent capable of causing disease.
Prevalence
The total number of existing cases of a disease in a population at a given point in time.
Vector (disease)
An organism (often an insect) that transmits a pathogen between hosts.

Practice questions

  • 3 marks

    Q1 Distinguish Distinguish between innate and adaptive immunity, and give one example of each.

    Show suggested answer

    Innate immunity is a non-specific, rapid defence present from birth that responds the same way to any pathogen — e.g. physical barriers such as skin and mucous membranes, or the inflammatory response. Adaptive immunity is a specific, slower defence that develops over an organism's lifetime and "remembers" particular pathogens — e.g. the production of memory B-cells and antibodies specific to a previously encountered pathogen.

  • 4 marks

    Q2 Explain Explain how the overuse of antibiotics can lead to an increase in antibiotic-resistant bacteria.

    Show suggested answer

    Within a bacterial population, random mutation may occasionally produce individuals with resistance to a particular antibiotic. When antibiotics are used, susceptible bacteria are killed, but any resistant individuals survive and are able to reproduce without competition from the susceptible strain. Overuse of antibiotics increases how often bacterial populations are exposed to this selection pressure, meaning resistant strains have more opportunities to survive, reproduce and become a larger proportion of the population over successive generations — an example of natural selection acting on a bacterial population as a direct result of human antibiotic use.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 Evaluate Evaluate the effectiveness of vaccination programs in controlling the spread of an infectious disease within a population.

    Show suggested answer

    Vaccination provides artificially acquired active immunity by exposing the immune system to a weakened, inactivated, or partial pathogen, triggering the production of specific antibodies and memory cells without causing the disease itself, so that a real infection is dealt with rapidly if encountered later. When vaccination coverage in a population is high enough, herd immunity is achieved — the pathogen struggles to find enough susceptible hosts to sustain transmission chains, indirectly protecting individuals who cannot be vaccinated (e.g. the immunocompromised). Historically, vaccination programs have been highly effective, contributing to the eradication of smallpox and large reductions in diseases such as measles and polio. However, effectiveness depends on maintaining high coverage rates, on the pathogen not mutating significantly enough to evade existing vaccines (as seen with seasonal influenza strains), and on equitable access to vaccines across populations. Overall, vaccination remains one of the most effective public health tools for infectious disease control, but its success is conditional on sustained, widespread uptake.
