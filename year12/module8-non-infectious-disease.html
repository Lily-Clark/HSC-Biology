Module 8: Non-infectious Disease and Disorders

The final HSC module: how organisms maintain a stable internal environment, what happens when homeostatic mechanisms fail, and how epidemiology is used to study non-infectious disease.

Syllabus dot points

Homeostasis

Inquiry question: How is an organism's internal environment maintained in response to a changing external environment?

  • Construct and interpret negative feedback loops that show homeostasis using a range of examples, including thermoregulation and blood glucose regulation.
  • Investigate the physiological adaptations of endotherms that assist in maintaining homeostasis in a particular external environment.

Causes and effects of non-infectious disease

Inquiry question: What causes non-infectious diseases and disorders?

  • Investigate causes and effects of non-infectious diseases and disorders, including genetic and environmental causes, and lifestyle factors.
  • Assess the appropriateness of technologies used to prevent or treat a named non-infectious disease.

Epidemiology

Inquiry question: How do epidemiological studies assist in the control of disease?

  • Analyse patterns of a non-infectious disease in a population with reference to its incidence and prevalence.
  • Evaluate the effectiveness of prevention and control strategies used for a named non-infectious disease.

Notes

What is homeostasis

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment despite changes in the external environment — e.g. regulating body temperature, blood glucose, water balance, and blood pH within a narrow set-point range. This stability is essential because most enzyme-driven metabolic processes only function efficiently within a narrow range of conditions.

Negative feedback loops

Homeostasis is maintained through negative feedback: a change in a variable is detected by a receptor, communicated (via nerves or hormones) to a control centre, which triggers an effector response that counteracts the original change, returning the variable towards its set point. This is distinct from positive feedback, which amplifies a change rather than reversing it (positive feedback is rare in homeostasis and is not itself a homeostatic mechanism). Being able to correctly label the receptor, control centre, effector and response in any given example is one of the most consistently tested skills in this module.

Thermoregulation in endotherms

Endotherms maintain a stable internal body temperature largely independent of the external environment, using physiological adaptations such as vasodilation/vasoconstriction of blood vessels near the skin, sweating or panting for evaporative cooling, shivering to generate heat through muscle contraction, and insulation (fur, fat) to reduce heat loss. Specific adaptations to a named environment (e.g. a desert or polar species) are a common HSC exam focus.

Causes of non-infectious disease

Non-infectious diseases can arise from genetic causes (e.g. inherited conditions such as cystic fibrosis), environmental causes (e.g. exposure to carcinogens or pollutants), or lifestyle factors (e.g. diet, physical activity, smoking) — and often from an interaction of more than one of these categories, as in type 2 diabetes, which involves both genetic predisposition and lifestyle factors. Distinguishing these causes matters because it changes which prevention or treatment strategies are appropriate.

Epidemiology

Incidence is the number of new cases of a disease in a population over a given time period, while prevalence is the total number of existing cases (new and pre-existing) at a given point in time. Epidemiological data on non-infectious disease is used to identify risk factors, evaluate whether a public health intervention (e.g. a screening program or public health campaign) has reduced disease burden over time, and to target prevention resources at the populations most affected.

Practice questions

  • 4 marks

    Q1 (Application). Using blood glucose regulation as an example, explain how negative feedback maintains homeostasis.

    Show suggested answer

    When blood glucose rises above the set point (e.g. after eating), this is detected by receptor cells in the pancreas, which respond by secreting insulin. Insulin stimulates cells to take up glucose and the liver to convert glucose to glycogen, lowering blood glucose back towards the set point. When blood glucose falls below the set point, the pancreas instead secretes glucagon, which stimulates the liver to break down glycogen into glucose, raising blood glucose levels. In both cases, the response acts to reverse the initial change and return the variable to its set point — the defining feature of a negative feedback loop.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 (Recall). Distinguish between the incidence and prevalence of a disease.

    Show suggested answer

    Incidence is the number of new cases of a disease that occur in a population within a specified time period. Prevalence is the total number of existing cases (both new and pre-existing) within a population at a particular point in time. A disease can have low incidence but high prevalence if it is long-lasting or chronic, since existing cases accumulate over time even as new cases remain rare.

  • 7 marks

    Q3 (Extended response — Evaluate). Evaluate the effectiveness of a named prevention or control strategy used to reduce the prevalence of a non-infectious disease in Australia.

    Show suggested answer

    Public health screening programs, such as mammography screening for breast cancer, aim to detect disease at an earlier, more treatable stage, reducing mortality rates by enabling earlier intervention. Skin cancer prevention campaigns (e.g. sun-safety public health messaging) target a key lifestyle/environmental risk factor — UV exposure — and have been associated with improved sun-protective behaviours and, over the longer term, changes in melanoma incidence trends in younger age groups. These strategies are generally effective at a population level, since they target well-established risk factors with strong supporting epidemiological evidence and are relatively low-cost to implement broadly. However, effectiveness is limited by factors such as uneven uptake across socioeconomic and geographic groups, the time lag before population-level effects on disease statistics become measurable, and the fact that no single strategy addresses every contributing cause of a multifactorial disease. A full evaluation should weigh the specific evidence for the named strategy's impact on incidence, prevalence or mortality data against these practical limitations, rather than simply describing what the strategy involves.
