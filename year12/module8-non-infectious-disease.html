Module 8: Non-infectious Disease and Disorders

The final HSC module: how organisms maintain a stable internal environment, what happens when homeostatic mechanisms fail, and how epidemiology is used to study non-infectious disease.

Syllabus checklist

Homeostasis

Inquiry question: How is an organism's internal environment maintained in response to a changing external environment?

  • Construct and interpret negative feedback loops that show homeostasis using a range of examples, including thermoregulation and blood glucose regulation.
  • Investigate the physiological adaptations of endotherms that assist in maintaining homeostasis in a particular external environment.

Causes and effects of non-infectious disease

Inquiry question: What causes non-infectious diseases and disorders?

  • Investigate causes and effects of non-infectious diseases and disorders, including genetic and environmental causes, and lifestyle factors.
  • Assess the appropriateness of technologies used to prevent or treat a named non-infectious disease.

Epidemiology

Inquiry question: How do epidemiological studies assist in the control of disease?

  • Analyse patterns of a non-infectious disease in a population with reference to its incidence and prevalence.
  • Evaluate the effectiveness of prevention and control strategies used for a named non-infectious disease.

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. Negative feedback loops and homeostasis

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment despite changes in the external environment — e.g. regulating body temperature, blood glucose, water balance and blood pH within a narrow set-point range. This stability matters because most enzyme-driven metabolic processes only function efficiently within a narrow range of conditions. Homeostasis is maintained through negative feedback: a change in a variable is detected by a receptor, communicated (via nerves or hormones) to a control centre, which triggers an effector response that counteracts the original change, returning the variable towards its set point. Being able to correctly label the receptor, control centre, effector and response — and to explain what happens when the variable rises versus falls — is one of the most consistently tested skills in this module. In blood glucose regulation: when blood glucose rises (e.g. after eating), receptor cells in the pancreas detect this and secrete insulin, which stimulates cells to take up glucose and the liver to convert it to glycogen, lowering blood glucose. When blood glucose falls, the pancreas instead secretes glucagon, prompting the liver to break down glycogen into glucose, raising blood glucose back up.

2. Physiological adaptations of endotherms

Endotherms maintain a stable internal body temperature largely independent of the external environment, using several physiological mechanisms. Vasodilation (widening of blood vessels near the skin) increases heat loss when the body is too warm; vasoconstriction (narrowing of these vessels) reduces heat loss when the body is too cold. Sweating (mammals) and panting (e.g. dogs, which sweat far less than humans) cause evaporative cooling. Shivering generates heat through rapid, involuntary muscle contraction. Insulation — fur, feathers, or subcutaneous fat — reduces the rate of heat loss to the environment. Specific named adaptations to a particular environment are a common HSC exam focus: e.g. the reduced surface area to volume ratio of animals in cold climates (Bergmann's rule) helping retain heat, or specialised counter-current heat exchange systems in the limbs of some cold-climate animals, which minimise heat loss to the extremities while still supplying them with blood.

3. Causes and effects of non-infectious disease

Non-infectious diseases can arise from genetic causes (e.g. inherited single-gene conditions such as cystic fibrosis, or genetic predisposition contributing to more complex conditions such as certain cancers), environmental causes (e.g. exposure to carcinogens, pollutants, or UV radiation), or lifestyle factors (e.g. diet, physical activity levels, tobacco or alcohol use). Many common non-infectious diseases arise from an interaction of more than one of these categories — type 2 diabetes, for instance, typically involves both a genetic predisposition and lifestyle factors such as diet and physical inactivity, rather than either cause alone being sufficient. Correctly distinguishing which category (or combination of categories) is driving a named disease matters because it determines which prevention or treatment strategies are actually appropriate — a purely genetic condition cannot be prevented by lifestyle change, whereas a lifestyle-driven condition may be substantially preventable.

4. Assessing technologies used to prevent or treat a named disease

Technologies for non-infectious disease span prevention (e.g. screening programs such as mammography for breast cancer, or genetic testing for inherited cancer risk genes), diagnosis (e.g. imaging technologies such as CT and MRI scans, blood tests for biomarkers), and treatment (e.g. insulin pumps and continuous glucose monitors for type 1 diabetes, dialysis for kidney failure, targeted cancer therapies). Assessing "appropriateness" means weighing a technology's effectiveness against practical considerations: cost and accessibility (does it reach the population who need it), invasiveness and risk (are there side effects or complications), and how well it fits the specific disease's biology (e.g. a screening test is only appropriate if early detection meaningfully improves outcomes for that disease). A strong response names a specific technology for a specific disease and works through these considerations explicitly, rather than asserting the technology is simply "good" or "effective."

5. Analysing incidence and prevalence patterns

As with infectious disease, incidence is the number of new cases of a non-infectious disease occurring in a population over a given time period, while prevalence is the total number of existing cases at a given point in time. For chronic non-infectious diseases (e.g. type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease), prevalence often rises over time even if incidence is stable, because affected individuals live with the condition for years or decades — analysing this distinction correctly is essential to interpreting public health data without being misled by rising case numbers that may simply reflect people living longer with a manageable condition, rather than the disease becoming more common. Epidemiological data is used to identify risk factors (by comparing incidence between groups with and without a suspected risk factor), evaluate whether an intervention has reduced disease burden over time, and target prevention resources toward the populations most affected.

6. Evaluating prevention and control strategies

Public health strategies for non-infectious disease include screening programs (early detection improving treatment outcomes, e.g. mammography, bowel cancer screening), public health campaigns targeting a specific risk factor (e.g. sun-safety messaging targeting UV exposure and skin cancer, or anti-smoking campaigns targeting lung cancer and cardiovascular disease), and policy interventions (e.g. taxation on tobacco, food labelling regulations). A genuine evaluation weighs demonstrated impact on incidence, prevalence or mortality data against practical limitations — such as uneven uptake across socioeconomic or geographic groups, the time lag before population-level effects become measurable in the data, and the fact that no single strategy addresses every contributing cause of a multifactorial disease. As with Module 6 and 7's evaluate-style dot points, always anchor the response to one specific, named disease and strategy, and finish with a clear overall judgement rather than an open-ended list of considerations.

Glossary

Effector
The part of a feedback system (e.g. an organ or gland) that produces a response to counteract a detected change.
Endotherm
An organism that maintains a stable internal body temperature largely independent of the external environment.
Epidemiology
The study of the patterns, causes and effects of disease in populations.
Homeostasis
The maintenance of a stable internal environment despite changes in the external environment.
Negative feedback
A regulatory mechanism in which a response counteracts an initial change, returning a variable to its set point.
Non-infectious disease
A disease not caused by a transmissible pathogen, arising instead from genetic, environmental or lifestyle factors.
Receptor (homeostasis)
A structure that detects a change in a variable being regulated, such as blood glucose or body temperature.
Vasoconstriction / vasodilation
The narrowing or widening of blood vessels, used to regulate heat loss from the body.

Practice questions

  • 4 marks

    Q1 Explain Using blood glucose regulation as an example, explain how negative feedback maintains homeostasis.

    Show suggested answer

    When blood glucose rises above the set point (e.g. after eating), this is detected by receptor cells in the pancreas, which respond by secreting insulin. Insulin stimulates cells to take up glucose and the liver to convert glucose to glycogen, lowering blood glucose back towards the set point. When blood glucose falls below the set point, the pancreas instead secretes glucagon, which stimulates the liver to break down glycogen into glucose, raising blood glucose levels. In both cases, the response acts to reverse the initial change and return the variable to its set point — the defining feature of a negative feedback loop.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 Distinguish Distinguish between the incidence and prevalence of a disease.

    Show suggested answer

    Incidence is the number of new cases of a disease that occur in a population within a specified time period. Prevalence is the total number of existing cases (both new and pre-existing) within a population at a particular point in time. A disease can have low incidence but high prevalence if it is long-lasting or chronic, since existing cases accumulate over time even as new cases remain rare.

  • 7 marks

    Q3 Evaluate Evaluate the effectiveness of a named prevention or control strategy used to reduce the prevalence of a non-infectious disease in Australia.

    Show suggested answer

    Public health screening programs, such as mammography screening for breast cancer, aim to detect disease at an earlier, more treatable stage, reducing mortality rates by enabling earlier intervention. Skin cancer prevention campaigns (e.g. sun-safety public health messaging) target a key lifestyle/environmental risk factor — UV exposure — and have been associated with improved sun-protective behaviours and, over the longer term, changes in melanoma incidence trends in younger age groups. These strategies are generally effective at a population level, since they target well-established risk factors with strong supporting epidemiological evidence and are relatively low-cost to implement broadly. However, effectiveness is limited by factors such as uneven uptake across socioeconomic and geographic groups, the time lag before population-level effects on disease statistics become measurable, and the fact that no single strategy addresses every contributing cause of a multifactorial disease. A full evaluation should weigh the specific evidence for the named strategy's impact on incidence, prevalence or mortality data against these practical limitations, rather than simply describing what the strategy involves.
