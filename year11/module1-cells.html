Module 1: Cells as the Basis of Life

This module is the foundation for every other module in the course. It looks at what distinguishes one cell from another, and how cells coordinate their internal and external activities.

Syllabus checklist

Cell structure

Inquiry question: What distinguishes one cell from another?

  • Analyse the structural features of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, and use the light microscope to make and record observations.
  • Investigate cell structures and organelles including the nucleus, ribosomes, mitochondria, chloroplasts, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, cell wall and cell membrane.
  • Investigate the impact of specialisation of cells for particular functions in animal and plant tissue via examination of prepared slides and micrographs.
  • Use models to represent the ultrastructure of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.

Cell function

Inquiry question: How do cells coordinate activities within their internal environment and the external environment?

  • Model the way in which materials move into and out of cells, including simple diffusion, osmosis and active transport.
  • Explain how the surface area to volume ratio of cells affects the rate of diffusion, and relate this to cell size and division.
  • Investigate the effect of changing the environment of a cell on the structure and functioning of the cell membrane, including changes in tonicity.
  • Model the structure of the cell membrane (fluid mosaic model) and relate this to its function.
  • Explain the significance of enzymes to cell function, including their structure, action, and factors affecting their activity (temperature, pH, substrate concentration).

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. Prokaryotic vs eukaryotic cell structure

Prokaryotic cells (bacteria and archaea) lack a true, membrane-bound nucleus — their genetic material sits free in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid. They also lack other membrane-bound organelles, are generally smaller (roughly 1–10 µm), have circular DNA, and may have additional structures such as a cell wall, capsule, flagella or pili. Eukaryotic cells (found in animals, plants, fungi and protists) are larger (10–100 µm), have a true nucleus enclosing linear DNA wrapped around histone proteins, and compartmentalise their functions into membrane-bound organelles. Using a light microscope, students should be able to identify basic structural differences (e.g. presence/absence of a nucleus) and calculate magnification and actual specimen size from a scale bar or given magnification. Remember the formula: magnification = image size ÷ actual size.

2. Cell structures and organelles

Each organelle's structure directly relates to its function — this link is the most commonly tested idea in this dot point. The nucleus houses DNA and controls gene expression via mRNA production. Ribosomes (free or bound to rough ER) synthesise proteins. Mitochondria have a folded inner membrane (cristae) that increases surface area for aerobic respiration and ATP production. Chloroplasts (plant/algal cells only) contain chlorophyll-rich thylakoid membranes for photosynthesis. The endoplasmic reticulum exists in rough (studded with ribosomes, protein processing) and smooth (lipid synthesis, detoxification) forms. The Golgi apparatus modifies, packages and ships proteins and lipids in vesicles. Lysosomes contain digestive enzymes for breaking down waste and worn-out organelles. The cell wall (plants, fungi, most prokaryotes — absent in animals) provides structural support and prevents osmotic lysis. The cell membrane regulates what enters and exits the cell.

3. Cell specialisation

Cells become specialised by expressing only the genes relevant to their function, altering their shape, organelle composition and surface features accordingly. Examples worth knowing by name: red blood cells (biconcave shape maximises surface area for gas exchange, no nucleus to maximise haemoglobin space), root hair cells (long thin extensions maximise surface area for water/mineral absorption), and neurons (elongated axon for rapid signal transmission over distance). When examining prepared slides or micrographs, students should be able to identify tissue type from structural clues (e.g. cell shape, presence of specific organelles) and link that structure back to its functional role within the organism.

4. Modelling cell ultrastructure

Models (physical, drawn, or digital) are used to represent the three-dimensional arrangement of organelles within a cell — this dot point is about being able to construct and interpret such models, not just label a pre-drawn diagram. A good model should show organelles roughly to scale relative to each other and to the cell as a whole, and should be able to represent both prokaryotic and eukaryotic ultrastructure clearly enough to compare them side by side.

5. Modelling membrane transport (diffusion, osmosis, active transport)

Simple diffusion is the passive net movement of small, non-polar or lipid-soluble particles (e.g. O₂, CO₂) from an area of high concentration to low concentration, continuing until equilibrium, and requiring no ATP. Osmosis is a special case of diffusion: the net movement of water molecules across a selectively permeable membrane from an area of high water potential (dilute solution) to low water potential (concentrated solution). Active transport moves substances against their concentration gradient (low to high), which requires ATP and specific membrane-bound carrier proteins (pumps) — this is how cells accumulate ions or nutrients even when the surrounding concentration is lower than inside the cell. Being able to draw a labelled diagram showing particle movement and correctly identifying which of the three mechanisms is occurring in a given scenario is a frequently tested skill.

6. Surface area to volume ratio and cell size

As a cell increases in size, its volume increases by the cube of its radius while its surface area increases only by the square of its radius — so volume grows proportionally faster than surface area, and the surface area to volume (SA:V) ratio falls. Because materials enter and exit a cell only across its surface (the membrane), a lower SA:V ratio means there is proportionally less membrane available to supply the cell's metabolic demands as it grows. This is the underlying reason cells divide rather than growing indefinitely larger, and why very active cells (e.g. those with high metabolic demand) tend to be small or have surface-area-increasing adaptations such as microvilli.

7. Tonicity and the effect of a changing environment on the cell membrane

A solution can be described relative to a cell's cytoplasm as hypotonic (lower solute concentration outside than inside — water moves into the cell by osmosis, potentially causing lysis in animal cells or turgor in plant cells), hypertonic (higher solute concentration outside — water moves out of the cell, causing crenation in animal cells or plasmolysis in plant cells), or isotonic (equal solute concentration — no net water movement). This dot point is usually assessed through a first-hand investigation (e.g. potato cores or red blood cells in a range of solute concentrations), so students should be comfortable both predicting outcomes and explaining unexpected results (e.g. why a cell wall prevents animal-style lysis in plant cells even in a strongly hypotonic solution).

8. The fluid mosaic model of the cell membrane

The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer — each phospholipid has a hydrophilic (water-attracting) phosphate head and two hydrophobic (water-repelling) fatty acid tails, which spontaneously arrange into a bilayer with heads facing the watery environments inside and outside the cell, and tails facing each other in the middle. Embedded within this bilayer are proteins (integral, spanning the membrane, or peripheral, attached to one surface) that act as channels, pumps, receptors or enzymes, plus cholesterol (which regulates membrane fluidity) and glycoproteins/glycolipids (involved in cell recognition). The model is called "fluid" because the phospholipids and proteins can move laterally within the layer, and "mosaic" because of the scattered, varied arrangement of embedded proteins. This structure directly explains selective permeability: small non-polar molecules pass easily through the bilayer, while larger or polar/charged molecules require specific transport proteins.

9. Enzymes: structure, action and factors affecting activity

Enzymes are globular proteins that act as biological catalysts, lowering the activation energy required for a biochemical reaction without being consumed or permanently altered themselves. Each enzyme has an active site with a specific three-dimensional shape that binds a complementary substrate — the induced-fit model describes how the active site changes shape slightly as it binds the substrate, improving the fit and catalytic efficiency (refining the older, more rigid "lock and key" model). Three factors are examinable in detail: temperature (activity increases with temperature up to an optimum, then falls sharply as heat disrupts the enzyme's tertiary structure and denatures it, permanently destroying the active site's shape); pH (each enzyme has an optimum pH, outside of which its structure — and therefore activity — is disrupted, sometimes reversibly, sometimes not); and substrate/enzyme concentration (increasing substrate concentration increases rate of reaction up to a point, after which all active sites are saturated and rate plateaus regardless of further substrate added).

Glossary

Active transport
Movement of particles across a membrane against their concentration gradient, requiring ATP and carrier proteins.
Denaturation
A permanent change to a protein's (e.g. enzyme's) three-dimensional structure, usually caused by heat or extreme pH, which destroys its function.
Diffusion
The passive net movement of particles from an area of high concentration to low concentration.
Eukaryotic cell
A cell containing a true, membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles.
Fluid mosaic model
The model describing the cell membrane as a phospholipid bilayer with embedded, mobile proteins.
Homeostasis
The maintenance of a stable internal environment despite external change (introduced here, developed fully in Module 8).
Hypertonic / hypotonic / isotonic
Terms describing a solution's solute concentration relative to a cell — higher, lower, or equal, respectively.
Organelle
A specialised structure within a cell that performs a specific function.
Osmosis
The net movement of water across a selectively permeable membrane from high to low water potential.
Prokaryotic cell
A cell without a true nucleus or membrane-bound organelles.
Surface area to volume ratio (SA:V)
The relationship between a cell's outer surface area and its internal volume, which decreases as a cell grows and limits maximum cell size.
Tonicity
The relative concentration of solutes in two solutions separated by a membrane, which determines the direction of osmotic water movement.

Practice questions

  • 2 marks

    Q1 Identify Identify two structural features that distinguish a prokaryotic cell from a eukaryotic cell.

    Show suggested answer

    Any two of: prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus (genetic material is in a nucleoid region); prokaryotic cells lack membrane-bound organelles; prokaryotic cells are generally smaller (1–10 µm vs 10–100 µm); prokaryotic cells have circular DNA rather than linear chromosomes.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 Explain Explain why an increase in cell size results in a decrease in the surface area to volume ratio, and outline the consequence of this for cell function.

    Show suggested answer

    As a cell increases in size, its volume increases by the cube of the radius while its surface area increases only by the square of the radius, so volume grows faster than surface area. This means there is proportionally less membrane available for the diffusion of nutrients in and wastes out relative to the cell's metabolic needs, which limits the maximum size a cell can reach before its metabolic demands outpace its capacity for exchange — driving cell division rather than continued growth.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 Explain / Working Scientifically A student places identical potato cores into four beakers containing different concentrations of sucrose solution (0%, 25%, 50%, 75%) and measures the mass of each core before and after 30 minutes. Explain what results you would expect, and justify the underlying cell biology.

    Show suggested answer

    The core in 0% sucrose (nearly pure water) is hypotonic relative to the potato cells, so water moves into the cells by osmosis and mass increases. As sucrose concentration rises, the solution becomes progressively more hypertonic relative to the cell's cytoplasm, so the direction of net water movement reverses — mass would be expected to decrease as water moves out of the cells and into the surrounding solution. Somewhere between these concentrations there should be an isotonic point where mass does not change, because there is no net water movement across the membrane. A full-mark response would also comment on experimental validity — e.g. controlling for core size/surface area, and repeating for reliability.
