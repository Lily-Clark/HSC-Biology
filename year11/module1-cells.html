Module 1: Cells as the Basis of Life

This module is the foundation for every other module in the course. It looks at what distinguishes one cell from another, and how cells coordinate their internal and external activities.

Syllabus dot points

Cell structure

Inquiry question: What distinguishes one cell from another?

  • Analyse the structural features of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, and use the light microscope to make and record observations.
  • Investigate cell structures and organelles including the nucleus, ribosomes, mitochondria, chloroplasts, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, cell wall and cell membrane.
  • Investigate the impact of specialisation of cells for particular functions in animal and plant tissue via examination of prepared slides and micrographs.
  • Use models to represent the ultrastructure of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.

Cell function

Inquiry question: How do cells coordinate activities within their internal environment and the external environment?

  • Model the way in which materials move into and out of cells, including simple diffusion, osmosis and active transport.
  • Explain how the surface area to volume ratio of cells affects the rate of diffusion, and relate this to cell size and division.
  • Investigate the effect of changing the environment of a cell on the structure and functioning of the cell membrane, including changes in tonicity.
  • Model the structure of the cell membrane (fluid mosaic model) and relate this to its function.
  • Explain the significance of enzymes to cell function, including their structure, action, and factors affecting their activity (temperature, pH, substrate concentration).

Notes

Cell theory

Modern cell theory rests on three ideas: all living things are made of one or more cells; the cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living things; and all cells arise from pre-existing cells. Keep this framework in mind — most of Module 1's content is really just unpacking what a cell needs to do to stay alive, and how its structure enables that.

Prokaryotic vs eukaryotic cells

Prokaryotic cells (bacteria and archaea) lack a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles — their genetic material sits in the cytoplasm in a nucleoid region. Eukaryotic cells (animals, plants, fungi, protists) are larger, more complex, and compartmentalise their functions into membrane-bound organelles. This compartmentalisation is a recurring exam theme: students should be able to explain why compartmentalisation is advantageous (e.g. it allows incompatible reactions to occur simultaneously, and increases the surface area for chemical reactions).

Membrane transport

Three mechanisms move materials across the membrane, and students consistently confuse them in exams:

  • Simple diffusion — passive movement of small, non-polar or lipid-soluble molecules (O₂, CO₂) down a concentration gradient, no energy required.
  • Osmosis — a special case of diffusion, specifically the movement of water across a selectively permeable membrane from high to low water potential.
  • Active transport — movement against a concentration gradient, requiring ATP and membrane proteins (pumps).

Surface area to volume ratio (SA:V) is the mechanism that links cell size to survival: as a cell grows, volume increases faster than surface area, which eventually limits the rate at which materials can diffuse in and waste can diffuse out relative to the cell's metabolic demand. This is the biological reason cells divide rather than simply growing larger.

Enzymes

Enzymes are globular proteins that lower the activation energy of biochemical reactions without being consumed. The lock-and-key and induced-fit models both describe how the enzyme's active site binds a specific substrate. Rate of reaction is affected by temperature (enzymes denature above their optimum), pH (denaturation outside the optimum range), and substrate/enzyme concentration (rate plateaus once all active sites are saturated).

Note to self: Add your own annotated diagrams here — a labelled fluid mosaic model and an enzyme-substrate diagram work well. Remember alt text for each: describe what the diagram shows, not just "diagram of cell membrane".

Practice questions

  • 2 marks

    Q1 (Recall). Identify two structural features that distinguish a prokaryotic cell from a eukaryotic cell.

    Show suggested answer

    Any two of: prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus (genetic material is in a nucleoid region); prokaryotic cells lack membrane-bound organelles; prokaryotic cells are generally smaller (1–10 μm vs 10–100 μm); prokaryotic cells have circular DNA rather than linear chromosomes.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 (Application). Explain why an increase in cell size results in a decrease in the surface area to volume ratio, and outline the consequence of this for cell function.

    Show suggested answer

    As a cell increases in size, its volume increases by the cube of the radius while its surface area increases only by the square of the radius, so volume grows faster than surface area. This means there is proportionally less membrane available for the diffusion of nutrients in and wastes out relative to the cell's metabolic needs, which limits the maximum size a cell can reach before its metabolic demands outpace its capacity for exchange — driving cell division rather than continued growth.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 (Extended response — Working Scientifically). A student places identical potato cores into four beakers containing different concentrations of sucrose solution (0%, 25%, 50%, 75%) and measures the mass of each core before and after 30 minutes. Explain what results you would expect, and justify the underlying cell biology.

    Show suggested answer

    The core in 0% sucrose (nearly pure water) is hypotonic relative to the potato cells, so water moves into the cells by osmosis and mass increases. As sucrose concentration rises, the solution becomes progressively more hypertonic relative to the cell's cytoplasm, so the direction of net water movement reverses — mass would be expected to decrease as water moves out of the cells and into the surrounding solution. Somewhere between these concentrations there should be an isotonic point where mass does not change, because there is no net water movement across the membrane. A full-mark response would also comment on experimental validity — e.g. controlling for core size/surface area, and repeating for reliability.
