Module 2: Organisation of Living Things

How cells are arranged into a hierarchical system — cells, tissues, organs, systems — and how nutrients, gases and other materials are transported around plants and animals.

Syllabus checklist

Organisation of cells

Inquiry question: How are cells arranged in a multicellular organism?

  • Distinguish the levels of organisation in the animal body, from cell to tissue to organ to system.
  • Perform a first-hand investigation to compare a plant and an animal cell, using a light microscope or image.

Nutrient and gas requirements

Inquiry question: What is the difference in nutrient and gas requirements between autotrophs and heterotrophs?

  • Compare autotrophs and heterotrophs in relation to the nutrients and gases they require, and the by-products they produce.
  • Explain the role of digestion in the intake of nutrients, and gas exchange in the intake of gases required for cellular processes.
  • Investigate the process of photosynthesis in terms of reactants and products, and factors affecting its rate.

Transport

Inquiry question: How does the composition of the transport medium change as it moves around the organism?

  • Model the structure and function of a range of specialised cells, tissues and organs in plants and animals related to nutrient and gas transport.
  • Analyse the impact on functioning of a system due to malfunction of one or more of its components, using an available case study.

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. Levels of organisation

Multicellular organisms are organised hierarchically to allow specialisation: individual cells with similar structure and function group together to form tissues (e.g. muscle tissue, epithelial tissue, xylem tissue); tissues working together for a common purpose form organs (e.g. the stomach, a leaf, the heart); and organs working together toward a shared overall function form systems (e.g. the digestive system, the vascular system in plants, the circulatory system). The advantage of this hierarchy is division of labour — individual cells can become highly efficient at one specific job rather than each having to perform every function needed for survival, which would be far less efficient at large body sizes.

2. First-hand investigation: comparing plant and animal cells

Using a light microscope (or high-quality images), students compare a plant cell (e.g. an onion epidermal cell or Elodea leaf cell) with an animal cell (e.g. a human cheek cell). Key observable differences: plant cells have a rigid cell wall (giving a fixed, regular shape), a large central vacuole, and often visible chloroplasts; animal cells have a flexible cell membrane only (giving an irregular shape), lack a cell wall and large central vacuole, and lack chloroplasts. This investigation should be written up with correct scientific drawing conventions: clear, single lines (no shading), labelled structures, a title, and a calculated or stated magnification.

3. Autotrophs vs heterotrophs

Autotrophs (e.g. plants, algae, some bacteria) synthesise their own organic nutrients from inorganic raw materials, most commonly via photosynthesis: they take in carbon dioxide and water and, using light energy captured by chlorophyll, produce glucose and release oxygen as a by-product. Heterotrophs (e.g. animals, fungi) cannot manufacture their own organic nutrients and must ingest other organisms; they take in oxygen for aerobic respiration and release carbon dioxide as a by-product. A precise exam answer always states the specific reactants, products, and energy source for each process, rather than simply naming the process.

4. Digestion and gas exchange

Digestion breaks down large, complex, often insoluble food molecules into small, soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream and used by cells — this is why heterotrophs require a digestive system while autotrophs do not. Effective gas exchange surfaces (alveoli in mammalian lungs, stomata in plant leaves, gills in fish) share a common set of structural features that maximise diffusion efficiency: a large surface area, a thin exchange surface (usually one cell thick, minimising diffusion distance), a moist surface (gases must dissolve to cross a membrane), and a good blood or transport supply (to maintain a steep concentration gradient by constantly removing the diffused gas).

5. Photosynthesis: reactants, products and rate-limiting factors

The overall word equation is: carbon dioxide + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen (symbol equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂). Photosynthesis occurs in the chloroplast, using chlorophyll to capture light energy. The rate of photosynthesis is limited by whichever factor is in shortest supply relative to the others — the syllabus expects discussion of light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, and temperature. Each produces a characteristic graph of rate against that factor: rate increases roughly linearly at first, then plateaus once another factor becomes limiting (this is the principle of limiting factors). Students should be able to sketch and interpret these graphs, and explain in words why a plateau occurs at a given point.

6. Specialised transport structures in plants and animals

Plants use two separate, specialised vascular tissues. Xylem transports water and dissolved mineral ions from roots to leaves and other tissues, in one direction only; movement is largely passive, driven by transpiration pull (evaporation of water from leaf stomata creates negative pressure that draws water up the xylem) supported by cohesion between water molecules and adhesion to xylem vessel walls. Phloem transports the products of photosynthesis (mainly sucrose) from source tissues (e.g. mature leaves) to sink tissues (e.g. roots, fruits, growing shoots) via active transport of sugars into phloem cells followed by pressure-driven bulk flow, and unlike xylem can move materials in either direction depending on the plant's current needs. Animals use a circulatory system built around specialised structures such as the heart (pumping), blood vessels (transport), and red blood cells (oxygen carrying via haemoglobin) — the specific circulatory arrangement (single vs double circulation, open vs closed) varies between animal groups, but all serve the same underlying purpose: moving nutrients, gases, hormones and wastes between exchange surfaces and body cells faster than diffusion alone could manage over large distances.

7. Impact of system malfunction (case study analysis)

This dot point asks you to trace how damage at one level of organisation (a cell or tissue) cascades upward to affect an entire system's function — a good case study names a specific condition and explains the mechanism, not just the symptoms. For example: in emphysema, damage to alveoli walls reduces the total surface area available for gas exchange, which reduces the rate of oxygen diffusion into the blood, which in turn reduces the amount of oxygen available for aerobic respiration in body cells, ultimately reducing exercise tolerance and overall energy availability for the organism. A strong response always draws this full causal chain from structural change through to whole-system consequence, rather than just stating "the person can't breathe properly."

Glossary

Autotroph
An organism that produces its own organic nutrients from inorganic raw materials, typically via photosynthesis.
Heterotroph
An organism that cannot produce its own organic nutrients and must obtain them by consuming other organisms.
Limiting factor
Whichever environmental factor (e.g. light, CO₂, temperature) is in shortest supply and therefore restricts the rate of a process such as photosynthesis.
Organ
A structure made of two or more tissue types working together to perform a specific function.
Phloem
Plant vascular tissue that transports the products of photosynthesis (mainly sucrose) between source and sink tissues.
System
A group of organs working together to carry out a broad life function, e.g. the digestive system.
Tissue
A group of similar cells working together to perform a shared function.
Transpiration
The evaporation of water from a plant's aerial parts (mainly through leaf stomata), which helps drive water movement up the xylem.
Xylem
Plant vascular tissue that transports water and dissolved minerals from roots to leaves, in one direction.

Practice questions

  • 3 marks

    Q1 Compare Compare the nutrient and gas requirements of an autotroph and a heterotroph.

    Show suggested answer

    Autotrophs (e.g. plants) require carbon dioxide, water and light energy to produce their own organic nutrients via photosynthesis, releasing oxygen as a by-product. Heterotrophs (e.g. animals) cannot synthesise their own organic molecules and instead require pre-formed organic nutrients obtained by consuming other organisms, along with oxygen for aerobic respiration, releasing carbon dioxide as a by-product.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 Outline Outline the role of xylem and phloem in a flowering plant.

    Show suggested answer

    Xylem transports water and dissolved mineral ions from the roots to the leaves and other parts of the plant, in one direction only, largely driven by transpiration. Phloem transports the products of photosynthesis (mainly sucrose) from source tissues (e.g. leaves) to sink tissues (e.g. roots, fruits, growing shoots), and can move materials in either direction depending on where they are needed.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 Explain / Working Scientifically A named case study shows that damage to alveoli (e.g. from emphysema) reduces a person's exercise tolerance. Explain how this malfunction at the cellular level affects the functioning of the whole respiratory system.

    Show suggested answer

    Alveoli are adapted for efficient gas exchange by providing a large surface area, thin walls (one cell thick) and a rich capillary blood supply, which together maximise the rate of diffusion of oxygen into the blood and carbon dioxide out of it. In emphysema, the walls of alveoli break down and merge, reducing the total surface area available for gas exchange. This reduces the rate at which oxygen can diffuse into the bloodstream and carbon dioxide can diffuse out, meaning less oxygen is delivered to respiring tissues for a given breath. During exercise, when the body's oxygen demand increases sharply, this reduced exchange capacity cannot keep pace, which is why a person with damaged alveoli experiences a much lower exercise tolerance than someone with healthy lung tissue — a clear example of how a structural change at the cellular/tissue level cascades up to affect the functioning of an entire system.
