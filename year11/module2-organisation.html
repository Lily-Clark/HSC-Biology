Module 2: Organisation of Living Things

How cells are arranged into a hierarchical system — cells, tissues, organs, systems — and how nutrients, gases and other materials are transported around plants and animals.

Syllabus dot points

Organisation of cells

Inquiry question: How are cells arranged in a multicellular organism?

  • Distinguish the levels of organisation in the animal body, from cell to tissue to organ to system.
  • Perform a first-hand investigation to compare a plant and an animal cell, using a light microscope or image.

Nutrient and gas requirements

Inquiry question: What is the difference in nutrient and gas requirements between autotrophs and heterotrophs?

  • Compare autotrophs and heterotrophs in relation to the nutrients and gases they require, and the by-products they produce.
  • Explain the role of digestion in the intake of nutrients, and gas exchange in the intake of gases required for cellular processes.
  • Investigate the process of photosynthesis in terms of reactants and products, and factors affecting its rate.

Transport

Inquiry question: How does the composition of the transport medium change as it moves around the organism?

  • Model the structure and function of a range of specialised cells, tissues and organs in plants and animals related to nutrient and gas transport.
  • Analyse the impact on functioning of a system due to malfunction of one or more of its components, using an available case study.

Notes

Levels of organisation

Multicellular organisms are organised hierarchically: cells with similar structure and function group together to form tissues (e.g. muscle tissue, epithelial tissue); tissues working together for a common purpose form organs (e.g. the stomach, a leaf); and organs working together form systems (e.g. the digestive system, the vascular system in plants). The advantage of this hierarchy is specialisation — cells can become highly efficient at one job rather than performing every function needed for survival.

Autotrophs vs heterotrophs

Autotrophs (e.g. plants, algae, some bacteria) synthesise their own organic nutrients from inorganic raw materials, most commonly via photosynthesis: they take in carbon dioxide and water, and — using light energy captured by chlorophyll — produce glucose and release oxygen. Heterotrophs (e.g. animals, fungi) cannot make their own organic nutrients and must ingest other organisms; they take in oxygen for aerobic respiration and release carbon dioxide as a by-product. A useful exam habit: always state the reactants, products, and where the energy comes from for each process, rather than just naming it.

Photosynthesis

The overall equation is: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Rate of photosynthesis is limited by whichever factor is in shortest supply relative to the others — light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, and temperature are the three the syllabus expects you to discuss, and each produces a characteristic rate-vs-factor graph that plateaus once another factor becomes limiting.

Digestion and gas exchange

Digestion breaks down large, complex food molecules into small, soluble molecules that can be absorbed and used by cells — this is why heterotrophs need a digestive system but autotrophs don't. Gas exchange surfaces (alveoli in mammal lungs, stomata in plant leaves, gills in fish) share common features that maximise diffusion efficiency: a large surface area, a thin exchange surface, a moist surface, and a good blood or transport supply to maintain a concentration gradient.

Transport systems

Plants use two separate vascular tissues: xylem, which transports water and dissolved minerals from roots to leaves (driven largely by transpiration pull), and phloem, which transports the products of photosynthesis (mainly sucrose) from source to sink, in either direction depending on the plant's needs. Animals use a circulatory system — the specific structure (open vs closed, single vs double circulation) varies between groups, but all serve the same purpose: moving nutrients, gases and wastes between exchange surfaces and body cells faster than diffusion alone could manage.

Practice questions

  • 3 marks

    Q1 (Application). Compare the nutrient and gas requirements of an autotroph and a heterotroph.

    Show suggested answer

    Autotrophs (e.g. plants) require carbon dioxide, water and light energy to produce their own organic nutrients via photosynthesis, releasing oxygen as a by-product. Heterotrophs (e.g. animals) cannot synthesise their own organic molecules and instead require pre-formed organic nutrients obtained by consuming other organisms, along with oxygen for aerobic respiration, releasing carbon dioxide as a by-product.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 (Recall). Outline the role of xylem and phloem in a flowering plant.

    Show suggested answer

    Xylem transports water and dissolved mineral ions from the roots to the leaves and other parts of the plant, in one direction only, largely driven by transpiration. Phloem transports the products of photosynthesis (mainly sucrose) from source tissues (e.g. leaves) to sink tissues (e.g. roots, fruits, growing shoots), and can move materials in either direction depending on where they are needed.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 (Extended response — Working Scientifically). A named case study shows that damage to alveoli (e.g. from emphysema) reduces a person's exercise tolerance. Explain how this malfunction at the cellular level affects the functioning of the whole respiratory system.

    Show suggested answer

    Alveoli are adapted for efficient gas exchange by providing a large surface area, thin walls (one cell thick) and a rich capillary blood supply, which together maximise the rate of diffusion of oxygen into the blood and carbon dioxide out of it. In emphysema, the walls of alveoli break down and merge, reducing the total surface area available for gas exchange. This reduces the rate at which oxygen can diffuse into the bloodstream and carbon dioxide can diffuse out, meaning less oxygen is delivered to respiring tissues for a given breath. During exercise, when the body's oxygen demand increases sharply, this reduced exchange capacity cannot keep pace, which is why a person with damaged alveoli experiences a much lower exercise tolerance than someone with healthy lung tissue — a clear example of how a structural change at the cellular/tissue level cascades up to affect the functioning of an entire system.
