Preliminary Exam Preparation

Year 11 doesn't count towards the ATAR, but it sets the content foundation for every HSC module — and for most schools, your Preliminary result determines whether you're recommended into the HSC course. Treat it seriously; future-you will thank you.

Before the exam

  • Work module by module, not randomly. Use this site's syllabus dot points as a checklist — for each one, test whether you can explain it out loud, without notes, in your own words. If you can't, that's your revision priority, not the topic you already feel confident in.
  • Build your own glossary as you go rather than only reading the one provided on each module page — the act of writing a definition in your own words is what makes it stick. Compare yours against the module glossary afterwards.
  • Practise past Preliminary topic tests from your school, and time yourself under exam conditions at least once before the real thing — reading speed and exam stamina are skills, not just knowledge.
  • Draw your own diagrams from memory (cell structure, membrane transport, food webs, feedback loops) rather than just re-reading labelled ones — recognising a diagram is a much easier task than reproducing one, and exams require the latter.
  • Use the flash cards for quick recall practice in short bursts (10–15 minutes) rather than one long cramming session — spaced repetition beats a single marathon revision session for retention.

Reading the question

  • Underline the command word (identify, describe, explain, compare) before you start writing — see the HSC Key Words & Verbs page if you're not sure what a word requires.
  • Check the mark allocation and use it to judge how much detail is expected — a 2-mark question wants two clear points, not a paragraph; a 6-mark question wants a fully developed response.
  • If the question gives you data, a diagram, or a scenario, use it. Markers are specifically looking for you to apply your knowledge to what's in front of you, not just recite a memorised answer that ignores the stimulus provided.

In the exam room

  • Skim the whole paper first and allocate your time roughly in proportion to the marks available, leaving a few minutes at the end to check your multiple choice and short-answer sections.
  • Answer the questions you're most confident about first to build momentum, then come back to harder ones — don't get stuck on one question and run out of time for the rest.
  • For extended response questions, jot a 30-second plan (3–4 keywords or a mini-structure) before writing — this prevents rambling and helps you notice if you're missing a key idea before you've committed to writing the whole response.
  • Use correct biological terminology throughout, not everyday language — "the cell takes in water" should become "water moves into the cell by osmosis," which shows the marker you understand the underlying mechanism.

Common Year 11 mistakes

  • Confusing diffusion, osmosis and active transport, or using them interchangeably instead of precisely.
  • Describing a structure ("the mitochondria is oval-shaped") when the question asked for function ("the mitochondria produces ATP through aerobic respiration").
  • Writing "evolves to" as though an individual organism changes during its own lifetime — remember natural selection acts on populations over generations, not on individuals responding to their environment.
  • Vague sampling answers that don't name a specific technique (quadrat, transect, mark-release-recapture) or justify why it suits the organism being studied.
