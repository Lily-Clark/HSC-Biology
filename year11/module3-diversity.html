Module 3: Biological Diversity

How environmental pressures shape species diversity, how adaptations improve survival, and the evidence supporting the Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection.

Syllabus checklist

Effects of the environment on organisms

Inquiry question: How do environmental pressures promote a change in species diversity and abundance?

  • Investigate the biological classification system, considering: features that identify major taxonomic groups, and factors used to classify species.
  • Assess the importance of species diversity and abundance in a given ecosystem or community.

Adaptations

Inquiry question: How do adaptations increase the organism's ability to survive?

  • Analyse structural, physiological and behavioural adaptations of species and evaluate the direct and indirect effects these adaptations have on the species' survival.

Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection

Inquiry question: What is the relationship between evolution and biodiversity?

  • Analyse evidence for the theory of evolution by natural selection.
  • Investigate types of evolution, including divergent and convergent evolution, and explain how species evolve as a result of gradual change over time.

Evidence for evolution

Inquiry question: What is the evidence that supports the Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection?

  • Investigate the evidence supporting the theory of evolution, including comparative anatomy, biochemistry (DNA, proteins), and the fossil record.

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. Classification systems

Biological classification groups organisms based on shared features, arranged hierarchically from broad to specific: Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species — a common mnemonic is "Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup." Major taxonomic groups are traditionally identified by observable features (e.g. presence of a backbone, type of symmetry, mode of reproduction), but modern classification increasingly relies on biochemical evidence (DNA and protein sequence similarity), because organisms can look superficially similar due to convergent evolution rather than shared ancestry. Species is generally defined as a group of organisms capable of interbreeding to produce fertile offspring.

2. Species diversity and abundance

Species diversity refers to the variety of species within an ecosystem (both the number of different species — species richness — and how evenly individuals are distributed among them — species evenness). Abundance refers to the number of individuals of a particular species within an area. Higher species diversity generally increases an ecosystem's stability and resilience to disturbance, because a wider range of species provides functional redundancy — if one population declines due to disease or environmental change, others can often fill its ecological role, reducing the risk of the whole ecosystem collapsing.

3. Structural, physiological and behavioural adaptations

Adaptations are inherited traits that improve an organism's chance of survival and reproduction in its specific environment. The syllabus separates these into three types: structural adaptations are physical features (e.g. the thick blubber of a whale for insulation in cold water, or the long neck of a giraffe for reaching high foliage); physiological adaptations are internal biochemical or metabolic processes (e.g. a cactus using CAM photosynthesis to open stomata only at night, minimising water loss in arid conditions); and behavioural adaptations are patterns of action (e.g. huddling behaviour in emperor penguins to conserve heat, or nocturnal activity to avoid daytime heat). A well-argued exam answer always links the specific adaptation to the specific environmental pressure it addresses and explains the survival advantage gained, rather than describing the adaptation in isolation. Direct effects improve survival immediately (e.g. camouflage reducing predation), while indirect effects may act via a secondary pathway (e.g. an adaptation that improves foraging efficiency, indirectly improving reproductive success through better body condition).

4. Evidence for natural selection: Darwin's observations

Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection rests on four connected observations: (1) individuals within a population show heritable variation in their traits; (2) populations have the capacity to produce more offspring than the environment can support with available resources; (3) this creates competition for limited resources (food, space, mates); and (4) individuals whose variations give them a survival or reproductive advantage in their environment are more likely to survive to reproductive age and pass those advantageous traits to their offspring. Over many generations, this differential survival and reproduction gradually shifts the frequency of traits within a population. Evolution is therefore a change in a population's inherited characteristics over time — it does not describe change within a single organism's lifetime, and organisms do not "choose" to evolve or develop traits because they need them.

5. Divergent and convergent evolution

Divergent evolution occurs when a single ancestral species gives rise to two or more descendant species that become increasingly different from each other over time, typically because they occupy different environments or ecological niches under different selection pressures (e.g. Darwin's finches on the Galapagos Islands, which diverged from a common ancestor into species with beaks suited to different food sources). Convergent evolution occurs when unrelated species independently evolve similar features because they face similar environmental pressures, not because of shared ancestry (e.g. the streamlined, torpedo-shaped bodies of sharks, dolphins and ichthyosaurs — a fish, a mammal, and an extinct reptile respectively — which each independently evolved this shape for efficient movement through water).

6. Evidence supporting the theory of evolution

Three main lines of evidence are examinable. Comparative anatomy distinguishes homologous structures (similar underlying bone arrangement inherited from a common ancestor, even if used for different functions — e.g. the pentadactyl limb pattern shared across vertebrates such as humans, whales, bats and dogs) from analogous structures (similar appearance/function due to convergent evolution, not shared ancestry — e.g. the wings of insects and birds). Biochemistry compares DNA and protein sequences between species: the more similar two species' sequences are, the more recently they are inferred to share a common ancestor, since mutations accumulate at a roughly predictable rate over time. The fossil record provides physical evidence of gradual change in species over geological time, including transitional forms that show intermediate features between two groups (such as Archaeopteryx, showing both reptilian and avian features), and the sequence of fossils in rock strata reflects the order in which species existed.

Glossary

Adaptation
An inherited trait that improves an organism's chance of survival and reproduction in its environment.
Analogous structure
A structure that is similar in function/appearance between species due to convergent evolution, not shared ancestry.
Classification
The organisation of living things into hierarchical groups based on shared features.
Convergent evolution
Independent evolution of similar traits in unrelated species due to similar environmental pressures.
Divergent evolution
The evolution of increasingly different traits in descendant species from a common ancestor.
Homologous structure
A structure with similar underlying anatomy, inherited from a common ancestor, that may serve different functions across species.
Natural selection
The process by which individuals with advantageous heritable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce, increasing the frequency of those traits in a population over generations.
Species diversity
The variety of species in an ecosystem, considering both the number of species and how evenly individuals are distributed among them.

Practice questions

  • 4 marks

    Q1 Distinguish Distinguish between structural, physiological and behavioural adaptations, providing one example of each.

    Show suggested answer

    A structural adaptation is a physical feature that improves survival — e.g. the thick fur of an Arctic fox for insulation. A physiological adaptation is an internal process that improves survival — e.g. a camel's ability to produce highly concentrated urine to conserve water. A behavioural adaptation is an action or pattern of behaviour that improves survival — e.g. migration of birds to more favourable climates. Each example should link the adaptation to a specific survival advantage in the organism's environment.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 Distinguish Distinguish between homologous and analogous structures, and explain what each provides evidence for.

    Show suggested answer

    Homologous structures have a similar underlying anatomical arrangement but may perform different functions, because they are inherited from a common ancestor (e.g. the pentadactyl limb in humans, whales and bats) — these provide evidence for divergent evolution from a shared ancestor. Analogous structures perform a similar function and may look superficially similar, but arose independently in unrelated lineages (e.g. the wings of insects and birds) — these provide evidence for convergent evolution rather than shared ancestry.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 Explain A population of insects contains individuals with a range of body colours, from pale to dark. Explain, using Darwin's theory of natural selection, how this population might change over time if the environment darkens (e.g. due to pollution darkening tree bark).

    Show suggested answer

    Within the population there is already variation in body colour, some of which is heritable. Before the environment changes, pale insects may be well camouflaged against light bark and better able to avoid predation, while dark insects are more visible and more likely to be eaten. Once the environment darkens, this selection pressure reverses: dark insects are now better camouflaged and more likely to survive to reproductive age and pass on their alleles for dark colouration, while pale insects are more likely to be predated before reproducing. Because more offspring are produced than the environment can support, this creates competition, and the survival advantage of dark individuals means their genes become more frequent in the next generation. Over many generations, this differential survival and reproduction should shift the population towards a higher proportion of dark individuals — a real historical example of this is industrial melanism in the peppered moth.
