Module 4: Ecosystem Dynamics

The final Preliminary module: how abiotic and biotic factors shape ecosystems, how ecologists sample populations, and how Australia's flora and fauna reflect past environmental change.

Syllabus checklist

Ecosystem dynamics

Inquiry question: What determines the diversity and structure of an ecosystem?

  • Investigate the abiotic and biotic factors within an ecosystem, and analyse how these factors relate to species diversity in that ecosystem.
  • Investigate and describe the flow of matter and energy through an ecosystem, including food webs, trophic levels, and energy transfer/loss.

Population sampling

Inquiry question: How do the activities of humans impact on the growth of a species?

  • Assess the range of technologies used by biologists to identify and monitor population growth over time, including sampling techniques such as quadrats and transects, and mark-release-recapture.

Past ecosystems and change

Inquiry question: What evidence is there for change in ecosystems over time?

  • Analyse the evidence for the evolutionary development and change of Australian biota over time, using at least one example.
  • Assess the impacts of a natural or human-induced event on a population, and possible actions to prevent the extinction of species.

Course content by syllabus dot point

1. Abiotic and biotic factors and species diversity

Abiotic (non-living) factors include temperature, rainfall, soil type and nutrients, light availability, water availability and pH. Biotic (living) factors include predation, competition (within and between species), disease, and symbiotic relationships (mutualism, commensalism, parasitism). Both categories interact to determine which species can survive in an ecosystem and in what numbers: species diversity tends to be higher where abiotic conditions are less extreme (e.g. stable temperature and reliable rainfall) and where biotic interactions such as competition and predation are balanced rather than overwhelming — extremely harsh abiotic conditions (e.g. deserts, polar regions) typically support fewer species than milder ones (e.g. tropical rainforests), because fewer species have the adaptations needed to survive them.

2. Food webs, trophic levels and energy flow

Energy enters most ecosystems as light energy, captured by producers (autotrophs) through photosynthesis and stored in glucose. This energy then flows through consumers at each successive trophic level: primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers, secondary consumers eat primary consumers, and tertiary consumers eat secondary consumers. A food web shows the interconnected feeding relationships between many species in an ecosystem, rather than a single linear food chain. At each transfer between trophic levels, roughly 90% of the energy available is lost — mainly as heat released during cellular respiration, with additional losses to undigested material and unused biomass (e.g. bones, fur) — meaning only around 10% is transferred into the biomass of the next trophic level. This is why food chains rarely extend beyond four or five levels, and why there is always far more available energy and biomass at the producer level than at the top of a food web (often represented as an energy or biomass pyramid).

3. Population sampling technologies

Ecologists use different sampling methods depending on the organism and habitat being studied. Quadrats (fixed-area square frames, often placed randomly or systematically) are suited to sampling stationary or slow-moving organisms such as plants, sessile intertidal animals, or fungi — species counts within each quadrat are used to estimate overall population density across a larger area. Transects are lines run across a habitat (often with quadrats placed at intervals along them) and are especially useful for showing how species distribution and abundance change across an environmental gradient, such as from a shoreline inland, or from full sun into shade. Mark-release-recapture is used for mobile animal populations: a sample is caught, marked in a way that doesn't harm the animal or affect its behaviour, and released; after time for redistribution, a second sample is taken, and the proportion of marked-to-unmarked individuals is used (via the Lincoln Index formula) to estimate total population size. Choosing and justifying the correct technique for a given organism is a frequently examined Working Scientifically skill.

4. Evidence of past ecosystem change and the evolution of Australian biota

Evidence for how ecosystems have changed over time comes from the fossil record, sedimentary rock layers (which preserve pollen, spores and other biological material in chronological order), and geological dating methods, together showing how species composition and climate have shifted over geological time. Australia's evolutionary story is a key case study: after separating from the Gondwanan supercontinent roughly 45–50 million years ago, Australian flora and fauna evolved in relative isolation, without the same competitive pressure from placental mammals that shaped evolution elsewhere — this allowed marsupials, already present in Gondwana, to diversify into a wide range of ecological niches largely undisturbed. Australia's increasingly nutrient-poor soils and arid, fire-prone climate over millions of years also drove distinctive adaptations in eucalypts, such as lignotubers (underground stem bases that resprout after fire) and fire-triggered seed release from woody seed capsules.

5. Human impacts and conservation actions

Human-induced events — habitat destruction and fragmentation, introduced/invasive species, climate change, pollution, and overharvesting — can reduce population size and genetic diversity, increasing a species' extinction risk. Natural events (e.g. bushfire, drought, volcanic eruption) can have similar impacts, though ecosystems often have some evolved resilience to natural disturbance that they may lack for novel human-caused pressures. Conservation strategies include habitat protection and restoration, captive breeding and reintroduction programs, control or eradication of invasive species, and legislation such as threatened species listings and protected area declarations. A strong exam answer for this dot point names a specific species or event and links the human impact directly to a measurable population or biodiversity effect, then evaluates whether a named conservation strategy has been effective in response.

Glossary

Abiotic factor
A non-living component of an ecosystem, such as temperature, rainfall or soil type.
Biotic factor
A living component of an ecosystem, such as predation, competition or disease.
Food web
A network of interconnected food chains showing feeding relationships between many species in an ecosystem.
Mark-release-recapture
A sampling technique for mobile animal populations, using the ratio of marked to unmarked individuals in a second sample to estimate population size.
Quadrat
A fixed-area sampling frame used to estimate the density of stationary or slow-moving organisms.
Transect
A line across a habitat used to sample how species distribution changes along an environmental gradient.
Trophic level
An organism's position in a food chain, defined by its feeding relationship (e.g. producer, primary consumer).

Practice questions

  • 3 marks

    Q1 Justify / Working Scientifically A student wants to estimate the population size of a mobile animal species, such as a fish population in a lake. Outline an appropriate sampling technique and explain why it is suitable.

    Show suggested answer

    Mark-release-recapture is appropriate for mobile species. A sample of individuals is caught, marked in a way that does not harm them or affect their behaviour, and released. After allowing time for the marked individuals to redistribute, a second sample is taken and the proportion of marked individuals is used (via the Lincoln Index) to estimate total population size. This technique is suitable because quadrats and transects rely on organisms staying within a fixed area, which does not hold for mobile species such as fish.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 Explain Explain why energy is progressively lost as it flows through successive trophic levels in a food chain.

    Show suggested answer

    At each trophic level, organisms use a large proportion of the energy they consume for their own cellular respiration and metabolic activity, releasing much of it as heat, and not all of an organism's biomass is consumed or digestible by the next consumer (e.g. bones, undigested material). As a result, only around 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is typically transferred to and incorporated into the biomass of the next level, with the rest lost primarily as heat.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 Explain Explain how Australia's geological isolation has contributed to the evolution of its distinctive flora and fauna, using at least one named example.

    Show suggested answer

    After separating from the Gondwanan supercontinent roughly 45–50 million years ago, Australia's flora and fauna evolved in relative isolation from other landmasses, without the same competitive pressure from placental mammals that shaped evolution elsewhere. This allowed marsupials, which had already diversified in Gondwana, to fill a wide range of ecological niches in Australia largely undisturbed — for example, radiating into forms analogous to those of placental mammals elsewhere, such as the marsupial mole and the (extinct) thylacine, an example of convergent evolution driven by similar ecological pressures despite no close relation to their placental counterparts. Similarly, Australia's nutrient-poor soils and increasingly fire-prone, arid climate over millions of years drove the evolution of eucalypts with adaptations such as lignotubers and fire-triggered seed release, allowing them to dominate the continent's vegetation. Fossil and pollen records provide evidence for these gradual changes in flora and fauna composition over this geological timescale.
