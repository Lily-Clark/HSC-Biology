Module 4: Ecosystem Dynamics

The final Preliminary module: how abiotic and biotic factors shape ecosystems, how ecologists sample populations, and how Australia's flora and fauna reflect past environmental change.

Syllabus dot points

Ecosystem dynamics

Inquiry question: What determines the diversity and structure of an ecosystem?

  • Investigate the abiotic and biotic factors within an ecosystem, and analyse how these factors relate to species diversity in that ecosystem.
  • Investigate and describe the flow of matter and energy through an ecosystem, including food webs, trophic levels, and energy transfer/loss.

Population sampling

Inquiry question: How do the activities of humans impact on the growth of a species?

  • Assess the range of technologies used by biologists to identify and monitor population growth over time, including sampling techniques such as quadrats and transects, and mark-release-recapture.

Past ecosystems and change

Inquiry question: What evidence is there for change in ecosystems over time?

  • Analyse the evidence for the evolutionary development and change of Australian biota over time, using at least one example.
  • Assess the impacts of a natural or human-induced event on a population, and possible actions to prevent the extinction of species.

Notes

Abiotic and biotic factors

Abiotic (non-living) factors include temperature, rainfall, soil type, light availability and pH; biotic (living) factors include predation, competition, disease and symbiotic relationships. Both interact to determine which species can survive in an ecosystem and in what numbers — species diversity tends to be higher where conditions are less extreme and where biotic interactions (such as competition) are less intense relative to available resources.

Food webs, trophic levels and energy flow

Energy enters most ecosystems as light energy, is captured by producers (autotrophs) via photosynthesis, and then flows through consumers at each successive trophic level (primary, secondary, tertiary consumers). At each transfer between trophic levels, roughly 90% of energy is lost — mostly as heat through cellular respiration — meaning only about 10% is available to the next level. This is why food chains rarely extend beyond four or five trophic levels, and why there is always far more biomass and energy at the producer level than at the top of a food web.

Sampling techniques

Ecologists use different sampling methods depending on the organism and habitat: quadrats (fixed-area frames) are suited to sampling stationary or slow-moving organisms such as plants; transects (lines across a habitat, often with quadrats placed along them) are useful for showing how species distribution changes across an environmental gradient; and mark-release-recapture is used for mobile animal populations, using the proportion of marked-to-unmarked individuals in a second sample to estimate total population size.

Evidence of ecosystem change over time

Evidence for how ecosystems have changed comes from the fossil record, sedimentary rock layers, and other geological dating methods, which together show how species composition and climate have shifted over geological time. Australia's isolation after separating from Gondwana is a key example used to explain the evolution of its unique flora and fauna (e.g. the diversification of marsupials in the relative absence of competing placental mammals, and eucalypt adaptations to fire-prone, nutrient-poor soils).

Human impacts and conservation

Human-induced events — habitat destruction, introduced species, climate change, pollution — can reduce population size and genetic diversity, increasing extinction risk. Conservation strategies include habitat protection and restoration, captive breeding programs, control of invasive species, and legislation (e.g. threatened species listings). A strong exam answer for this dot point names a specific species or event and links the human impact directly to a measurable population or biodiversity effect.

Practice questions

  • 3 marks

    Q1 (Working Scientifically). A student wants to estimate the population size of a mobile animal species, such as a fish population in a lake. Outline an appropriate sampling technique and explain why it is suitable.

    Show suggested answer

    Mark-release-recapture is appropriate for mobile species. A sample of individuals is caught, marked in a way that does not harm them or affect their behaviour, and released. After allowing time for the marked individuals to redistribute, a second sample is taken and the proportion of marked individuals is used (via the Lincoln Index) to estimate total population size. This technique is suitable because quadrats and transects rely on organisms staying within a fixed area, which does not hold for mobile species such as fish.

  • 3 marks

    Q2 (Recall). Explain why energy is progressively lost as it flows through successive trophic levels in a food chain.

    Show suggested answer

    At each trophic level, organisms use a large proportion of the energy they consume for their own cellular respiration and metabolic activity, releasing much of it as heat, and not all of an organism's biomass is consumed or digestible by the next consumer (e.g. bones, undigested material). As a result, only around 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is typically transferred to and incorporated into the biomass of the next level, with the rest lost primarily as heat.

  • 6 marks

    Q3 (Extended response). Explain how Australia's geological isolation has contributed to the evolution of its distinctive flora and fauna, using at least one named example.

    Show suggested answer

    After separating from the Gondwanan supercontinent roughly 45–50 million years ago, Australia's flora and fauna evolved in relative isolation from other landmasses, without the same competitive pressure from placental mammals that shaped evolution elsewhere. This allowed marsupials, which had already diversified in Gondwana, to fill a wide range of ecological niches in Australia largely undisturbed — for example, radiating into forms analogous to those of placental mammals elsewhere, such as the marsupial mole and the (extinct) thylacine, an example of convergent evolution driven by similar ecological pressures despite no close relation to their placental counterparts. Similarly, Australia's nutrient-poor soils and increasingly fire-prone, arid climate over millions of years drove the evolution of eucalypts with adaptations such as lignotubers and fire-triggered seed release, allowing them to dominate the continent's vegetation. Fossil and pollen records provide evidence for these gradual changes in flora and fauna composition over this geological timescale.
