HSC Key Words & Verbs

NESA exam questions are built around specific command words, and each one signals a different depth and style of response. Misreading the verb is one of the most common — and most avoidable — ways students lose marks. Roughly, the verbs below are ordered from lowest to highest cognitive demand.

Note to self: This table paraphrases the general intent of NESA's official glossary of key words for a Biology context. For the definitive wording used across all HSC subjects, see NESA's published glossary of key words.
Common HSC command words, from lowest to highest demand
WordWhat it requiresExample stem
Recall / State / IdentifyProvide a fact, term, or short piece of information with no explanation needed.Identify the organelle responsible for ATP production.
DefineState the precise meaning of a term.Define homeostasis.
ListProvide a number of points with no elaboration, usually one per line.List three abiotic factors that affect species distribution.
OutlineGive a brief summary of the main points — more than a list, but without full explanation of cause and effect.Outline the process of DNA replication.
DescribeGive a detailed, structured account of something — what it is, what happens, what it looks like — without necessarily explaining why.Describe the structure of the cell membrane.
ExplainGive reasons for, or causes of, something — a "why" or "how" answer, showing the underlying mechanism or relationship.Explain how surface area to volume ratio limits cell size.
Distinguish / Compare / ContrastIdentify similarities and/or differences between two or more things, usually in a structured, side-by-side way.Distinguish between mitosis and meiosis.
Account forExplain why something is the case, often linking a result back to underlying reasons.Account for the change in enzyme activity above 40°C.
AnalyseBreak something down into its parts and examine how they relate to each other, to draw out patterns, causes, or implications.Analyse the relationship between mutation and antibiotic resistance in a bacterial population.
JustifySupport a particular position, decision or conclusion with reasoning and evidence.Justify the use of mark-release-recapture for sampling a fish population.
AssessMake a judgement about the value, significance or extent of something, based on evidence.Assess the importance of genetic variation for a population's long-term survival.
EvaluateMake a judgement based on criteria, weighing up the strengths and limitations of something — usually the highest-demand word, requiring a balanced, evidence-based conclusion.Evaluate the effectiveness of vaccination programs in controlling infectious disease.
PredictState a likely outcome based on available information or a known pattern, without necessarily being asked to justify it.Predict the phenotype ratio of the offspring from this cross.
InterpretDraw meaning from information given (data, a graph, a diagram) — explain what it shows.Interpret the trend shown in the graph of enzyme activity against pH.
InvestigatePlan, carry out, or describe a process for researching or testing an idea, often referring to first-hand or secondary-sourced information.Investigate the effect of temperature on the rate of photosynthesis.
ModelDemonstrate understanding using a diagram, simulation, or other representation of a structure or process.Model the process of natural selection acting on a population.
ProposePut forward a plan, explanation, or idea for consideration.Propose a strategy to reduce antibiotic resistance in a hospital setting.

Common mistakes

  • Treating "outline" or "describe" questions like "explain" questions (or vice versa) — describing what happens is not the same as explaining why it happens, and markers award marks based on which was actually asked for.
  • Answering "evaluate" or "assess" questions with pure description — these words require a judgement, supported by evidence, usually with a concluding statement. A response that only describes the topic without weighing it up will be capped well below full marks regardless of how accurate the content is.
  • Ignoring the mark allocation. As a rough guide, plan for about one distinct idea per mark in short-answer questions — a 6-mark "explain" question needs roughly six developed points, not one long paragraph repeating the same idea.
